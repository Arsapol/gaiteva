# Substance Dossier: Magnesium glycerophosphate

## 1. Identity
Magnesium glycerophosphate (magnesium salt of glycerophosphoric acid; mixture of magnesium (RS)-2,3-dihydroxypropyl phosphate and 2-hydroxy-1-(hydroxymethyl)ethyl phosphate isomers). CAS 927-20-8. Molecular formula C3H7MgO6P (anhydrous), MW ~194.4 g/mol; commercial grade is a hydrate. Synonyms: magnesium alpha-glycerophosphate, magnesium 1-glycerophosphate, glycerophosphoric acid magnesium salt, MgGP, MGP. Elemental Mg content per EP/BP monograph 11.0-12.5% (Tier 2, Mubychem/Anmol EP-BP datasheet; ~12.37% nominal). NOTE: CAS 1319-70-6 is POTASSIUM glycerophosphate, not this substance. In the CURRENT formula it appears ONLY in the dry CAPSULES A/B/C (60/80/70 mg, dry solid) — it is NOT in any liquid SKU.

## 2. Three-rule findings
- **R1 solubility:** SLIGHTLY SOLUBLE IN WATER. EP/BP monograph descriptor: "slightly soluble in water" + "freely/dissolves in dilute solutions of acids" + "practically insoluble in alcohol (96%)" (Tier 2: Mubychem/Anmol/Glycerophosphate.com EP-BP SDS; PubChem CID 10012877). Manufacturer Dr. Paul Lohmann classifies aqueous solubility as "−− < 1 g/L" (Tier 2 datasheet). "Slightly soluble" in pharmacopeial scale = 1 g in 100-1000 mL water (~1-10 g/L). The often-quoted "1 g in 50 mL" (=~20 g/L) clarity is achieved ONLY when co-dissolved with added glycerophosphoric acid, and even then yields a "minimally opalescent" (slightly cloudy) solution after 16 h (Tier 2/3: patent WO2017089595A1; ChemicalBook 927-20-8). Pure salt in plain near-neutral water gives an opalescent/turbid solution — EP QC explicitly limits opalescence of the test solution. HEADROOM at intended use: in the CURRENT formula this salt is DRY (capsule, 60-80 mg), so R1 is NOT applicable in solid form (solubility moot for a swallowed capsule). IF anyone attempted to dissolve it in the ~100-250 mL concentrate liquids to deliver tens of mg Mg, intended conc (~0.6-3 g/L of salt) would meet or exceed the ~1 g/L near-neutral ceiling -> headroom <1 -> BLOCK for liquid use unless acidified with free glycerophosphoric acid/citric acid (the formula's mild citrate/citric system is NOT sufficient to fully clarify it and risks Mg-phosphate precipitation). For the redesign the soluble drink Mg source is MgCl2 (~555-560 g/L); MgGP stays a DRY (feed/capsule) form only.
- **R2 stability:** Solid: stable under normal dry storage; HYGROSCOPIC (white amorphous powder) -> caking, store sealed/desiccated (Tier 2: Lohmann, ChemicalBook 927-20-8). In aqueous solution the glycerophosphate ester is hydrolysable to glycerol + inorganic phosphate, rate increasing with low pH, heat and phosphatase enzymes (Tier 2: ChemicalBook/patent literature WO2017089595A1) — at the formula's pH ~5.8-6.2 and ambient temp chemical hydrolysis is slow but non-zero over months. The dominant in-solution failure mode is NOT hydrolysis but PHYSICAL instability: opalescence/turbidity and slow deposit formation; clear, stable aqueous solutions over 2-3 months at 5-25C (ICH) were achievable ONLY with a 1:1 molar glycerophosphoric-acid + glycerol co-formulation (Tier 2/3 patent WO2017089595A1), not in a plain mildly-acidic citrate drink. No water = none of these problems: dry capsule/feed avoids hydrolysis, turbidity, and microbial growth entirely; this is why the winning design keeps Mg dry. Not notably light- or oxygen-sensitive. Preservative note: irrelevant in the dry form; in any wet form K-sorbate is only ~6% active at pH 6 (sorbic acid pKa ~4.76), an independent BLOCK-level defect of wet SKUs — another reason to keep Mg dry.
- **R3 conflicts:** Mg(2+) + phosphate/carbonate -> insoluble magnesium phosphate precipitate (turbid/cloudy) — magnesium glycerophosphate itself can throw inorganic phosphate on partial hydrolysis, which then co-precipitates Mg; this is the mechanistic root of its opalescence (Tier 2: EPS "Phosphorus and foggy water"; general Mg-phosphate insolubility). Pair -> mechanism -> mitigation: (1) MgGP + citrate/phosphate buffers (Na/K citrate present in S2-S5) -> Mg can chelate citrate (mild, keeps Mg in solution) BUT free phosphate from the salt precipitates -> mitigation: do NOT put MgGP in a phosphate/high-citrate liquid; use MgCl2 for any drink. (2) MgGP + divalent-sensitive actives (none critical here) -> minimal. (3) Bitter/astringent taste of Mg salts -> palatability hit in a drink -> mitigation: keep dry capsule/feed. (4) DEB: glycerophosphate is a weak/near-neutral anion (vs Cl) — contributes little to chloride balance; for the near-neutral Cl-balanced fight-day drink, MgGP's phosphate-side anion is NOT preferred; MgCl2 supplies the needed Cl. Physiological: see R3 of MgCl2 dossier for the shared Mg laxative ceiling.

## 3. Avian/poultry evidence
POULTRY Mg requirement ~500-600 mg/kg feed (NRC 1994, "does not exceed 0.6 g/kg DM"; Merck Vet Manual Nutritional Requirements of Poultry, Tier 1). Toxicity is ENTERIC/cathartic, NOT a blood-Mg neuromuscular ceiling: excess dietary Mg causes osmotic diarrhoea/wet litter; explicit harm shown only at high inclusions (e.g. ~1.4% MgCO3 -> skeletal abnormalities/diarrhoea) — the practical laxative threshold is ~0.3-0.5% feed (3000-5000 mg/kg) (Tier 1: World's Poultry Sci J magnesium-in-poultry review, Cambridge Core; ScienceDirect S0032579119395355 Mg-source digesta/excreta moisture study; MendelNet 2016 dietary Mg). Mg supplementation (organic and inorganic forms) improved redox status and reduced breast myopathy in heat/dietary-stressed broilers (PMC6826508, Tier 1) — supports a chronic FEED role for Mg, consistent with placing it in the dry preload. NO avian study was found specifically on magnesium GLYCEROPHOSPHATE as the Mg source in poultry — its avian use is inferred from generic dietary-Mg evidence (gap). Form is uricotelic-friendly: freely-excreted Mg excess is renally cleared; the binding constraint is gut osmotic laxation, not blood Mg. Per-session delivered Mg from a 60-80 mg capsule (~7.4-9.9 mg elemental Mg at 12.4%) is trivial and far below the laxative threshold.

## 4. Verdict
ADD-conditional as a DRY (capsule/feed) Mg source ONLY — SKIP/BLOCK for any liquid drink. (1) PRELOAD (dry feed): acceptable Mg carrier, but for a feed premix cheaper high-loading salts (MgO ~60% Mg, MgCl2, or MgSO4) deliver more elemental Mg per gram and MgGP's only edge (lower laxation, higher bioavailability — Tier 3 marketing, PMC6683096 human data) is marginal in a uricotelic bird; keep total feed Mg under the ~0.3-0.5% enteric ceiling. (2) FIGHT-DAY DRINK: SKIP — sparingly soluble (<~1 g/L near-neutral), opalescent, and precipitation-prone with the citrate/phosphate matrix; use MgCl2 instead (or, per winning design, deliver NO Mg in the drink and keep all Mg in feed). (3) CURRENT capsules A/B/C: the 60/80/70 mg dry dose is fine as-is (dry, ~7-10 mg elemental Mg/cap, no solubility/stability problem). Net: retain only as a DRY ingredient; do not migrate it into solution.

## 5. Cautions
Welfare/safety/producibility notes (non-blocking): (1) PRODUCIBILITY — a non-chemist who tries to dissolve magnesium glycerophosphate in water will get a persistently cloudy/opalescent drink and may assume it "didn't work" or add acid uncontrollably; keep it strictly dry to make mixing idiot-proof. (2) Marketing claims of "high solubility / highly bioavailable / non-laxative" (PEScience, Nootropics Depot, Synchronicity — Tier 3) directly CONTRADICT the EP/BP monograph and Lohmann datasheet "slightly soluble / <1 g/L"; do NOT design a liquid around the marketing claim. (3) Stacking Mg across SKUs: the legacy formula already carries ~619 mg Mg in S5 plus Mg-malate in S2/S3 and MgCl2; summing capsule MgGP on top must stay under ~150 mg elemental Mg/session and feed under the enteric ceiling — monitor droppings/wet litter on scale-up. (4) Hygroscopicity -> package the dry premix with desiccant. (5) Bitter taste of Mg salts can depress drink intake -> another reason to keep Mg in feed, not the drink.
