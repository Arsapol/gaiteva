# Substance Dossier: Glucose/sucrose drink sugar (palatability)

## 1. Identity
D-Glucose (dextrose) — monohydrate CAS 5996-10-1 (MW 198.17), anhydrous CAS 50-99-7 (MW 180.16); reducing aldohexose monosaccharide; SGLT1 substrate. Sucrose (table sugar, saccharose) CAS 57-50-1, MW 342.30; a NON-reducing disaccharide of glucose+fructose (glycosidic bond ties up both anomeric/reducing carbons). Synonyms: dextrose/grape sugar/blood sugar (glucose); saccharose/cane or beet sugar (sucrose). In this formula the relevant role is as the palatability/caloric sugar of the fight-day ORS drink; the design uses dextrose ~20 g anhydrous (~22 g monohydrate) per 1 L = glucose ~111 mM (0.111 M). Sucrose is evaluated as a candidate alternative/palatability sweetener.

## 2. Three-rule findings
- **R1 solubility:** GLUCOSE: ~909 g/L (~91 g/100 mL water @25C) [Tier 2, multiple references incl. NIST/textbook]. Intended ~20 g anhydrous/L → headroom = 909/20 ≈ 45× → OK (far below saturation; dry-stick reconstitution trivially dissolves). Note dextrose MONOHYDRATE has slightly lower effective anhydrous-equivalent solubility but still hundreds of g/L; not limiting. SUCROSE: ~2000 g/L (~200 g/100 mL @25C, i.e. ~2.0 g/g water) [Tier 2]; even higher than glucose, so if sucrose were substituted at equivalent sweetness/calorie it would also be OK (headroom >>3). VERDICT R1: OK for both at the trivial ~6 g/L total-solids drink loading — solubility is not a constraint for either sugar dry-stick reconstituted in 1 L cool water.
- **R2 stability:** GLUCOSE is a REDUCING sugar → its open-chain aldehyde drives (a) Maillard/non-enzymatic browning with free amino groups (amino acids, peptides, amine vitamins like B6) and (b) caramelization at high temp; browning is negligible below ~pH 6 and slow at ambient temp but accelerates with pH, temperature, and storage time [Maillard kinetics: J Agric Food Chem / Food Chem reviews, Tier 2; pH-dependence "little if any browning below pH 6"]. At the drink's acidified pH ~3.8-4.2 and make-fresh single-day use, glucose Maillard is minimal IN ISOLATION, but glucose is highly browning-reactive once amines are co-present (this is exactly why the design keeps amino acids/ascorbate/B-vitamins OUT of the dextrose stick). SUCROSE is a NON-reducing disaccharide → it does NOT undergo Maillard directly, BUT it acid-hydrolyzes ("inversion") to glucose+fructose, and the rate rises exponentially as pH falls; activation energy ~99 kJ/mol for acid hydrolysis (pronounced >80C) [sucrose inversion kinetics, Tier 2]. So at the drink's low pH ~3.8-4.2, sucrose would slowly invert to reducing sugars over storage — but for a make-fresh single-day acidified drink at ambient temp this inversion is small. Microbial: any sugar solution + warmth supports microbial growth → make-fresh-daily and discard after the fight-day session is the governing control (the dry-stick/acidified design removes hydrolysis, Maillard, microbial and preservative-at-pH-6 problems). Realistic shelf-life: DRY sugar = effectively indefinite if kept desiccated; reconstituted drink = single day, discard same-day. Not light/O2 sensitive itself. Stable pH window vs formula ~5.8-6.2: glucose physically stable across that range (browning needs amines + time/heat); fine.
- **R3 conflicts:** Glucose↔amino acids/amines (β-alanine, L-citrulline, taurine, betaine has a quaternary-amine so low reactivity, P5P/B6, B-complex) → MAILLARD browning + actives loss on storage with warmth; mechanism = aldehyde-amine condensation; MITIGATION = keep reducing sugar separate from free amino groups, make-fresh, acidify, ambient temp (this is why the redesign deletes the S2 corn-syrup+maltodextrin+ribose+ascorbate stack and isolates dextrose). Glucose↔sodium ascorbate → ascorbate degradation accelerated in reducing-sugar matrix at near-neutral pH; MITIGATION = give vitamin C dry/separate, never co-mixed (per design). Glucose+Na (NaCl) → SYNERGY not conflict: SGLT1 glucose+Na cotransport is the intended mechanism; glucose:Na molar ~1.8-2.1:1 optimal. No precipitation/chelation issues (sugars are non-ionic, do not chelate Ca/Mg/Zn/Fe). Sucrose-specific: at low drink pH it inverts to reducing sugars, then inherits glucose's Maillard reactivity — so sucrose offers no Maillard advantage in an acidified drink and adds fructose (which chickens REJECTED at high dose). NET: dextrose ALONE in the dry stick has no R3 conflict at intended loading; conflicts only arise if amines/ascorbate are co-packed → design already separates them.

## 3. Avian/poultry evidence
DIRECT avian palatability evidence — Higashida et al. 2022, Poult Sci 101(8):101928, PMID 35679679, DOI 10.1016/j.psj.2022.101928 [Tier 1]: chickens LACK the T1R2 sweet-receptor gene, yet in two-bottle choice tests showed a SLIGHT PREFERENCE for 0.2 M glucose and 0.1 M sucrose vs water (P<0.05), with sweet sensing via a T1R2-INDEPENDENT pathway (SGLT1 + ATP-gated K+ channels expressed in palate; galactose preference blocked dose-dependently by an SGLT1 inhibitor; GLP-1/GLP-1R present orally). CRITICAL DOSE WINDOW: high doses REJECTED — 1.0 M sucrose aversion (P<0.05) and 1.0 M fructose aversion (P<0.01). The design drink glucose ~111 mM (0.111 M) sits squarely in the mild-preference / accepted band (between 0.05 and 0.2 M), NOT the rejected ≥1.0 M zone → palatability supported, not penalized. Functional (non-palatability) avian support for glucose: SGLT1 glucose+Na cotransport drives water uptake (Shibata 2023 Poult Sci PMID 37321034 [Tier 1], with caveat SGLT1 attenuates toward market age ~5 wk); 4% glucose water stabilized hematocrit/osmolality under heat (Zhou 1998 Poult Sci 77(5):644-7 PMID 9603349 [Tier 1]). Birds are uricotelic with naturally high, diet-insensitive blood glucose → value is hydration/osmolality and oral acceptance, NOT a glycemic "spike". NO avian-specific MAX-tolerable oral sugar toxicity threshold found beyond the behavioral 1.0 M rejection — treat ≥1.0 M as the palatability ceiling, not a toxicity ceiling (UNKNOWN true toxic dose).

## 4. Verdict
ADD-strong (as DEXTROSE/D-glucose) — FIGHT-DAY DRINK only, ~20 g anhydrous (~22 g monohydrate) per 1 L → glucose ~111 mM (0.111 M). Serves dual role: (1) SGLT1 glucose+Na cotransport rehydration backbone, (2) palatability/acceptance lever within the chicken-preferred 0.1-0.2 M window (PMID 35679679). Keep glucose:Na molar ~1.8-2.1:1. SKIP SUCROSE as the primary sugar: it is non-reducing (a stability plus) but in an acidified low-pH drink it inverts to glucose+fructose, gains no real Maillard advantage, and adds fructose which chickens REJECT at high dose — dextrose is the cleaner, directly-SGLT1-relevant choice. Make-fresh daily, acidified, single-day discard; keep dextrose stick free of amino acids/ascorbate/B-vitamins to prevent Maillard. Do NOT push concentration toward 1.0 M (palatability aversion). Net: dextrose ADD-strong; sucrose SKIP.

## 5. Cautions
1) Palatability ceiling is real, not just hydration: chickens REJECT ≥1.0 M sugar (sucrose P<0.05, fructose P<0.01; PMID 35679679) — over-concentrating the drink to deliver more calories/electrolytes would both break the <300 mOsm hypotonic target AND trigger taste aversion → reduced intake → LESS hydration. Stay near 0.1-0.2 M glucose. 2) Hydration only works if the bird DRINKS — under fight-stress voluntary intake may be poor; sugar improves acceptance but does not guarantee it (observational; verify field intake). 3) Microbial safety of any wet sugar solution: make-fresh, acidify (~pH 4), discard same-day; never store reconstituted drink warm for hours. 4) Maillard discipline: never co-pack dextrose with amino acids (citrulline, taurine), ascorbate, or B-vitamins — keep the sugar stick "clean". 5) Sucrose-specific: if a handler substitutes table sugar, low-pH inversion yields fructose, the one sugar chickens actively rejected at high dose — discourage sucrose substitution. 6) WELFARE NOTE (non-blocking): scope is the bird's own rehydration/acceptance; a palatable hypotonic SGLT1 ORS is welfare-positive (encourages voluntary drinking, better rehydration) — no welfare downside to the sugar itself at these doses.
