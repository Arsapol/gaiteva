# Substance Dossier: Maltodextrin (DE 10-12)

## 1. Identity
Maltodextrin DE 10-12. A non-sweet white powder of D-glucose polymers (alpha-1,4 and alpha-1,6 linked) produced by partial enzymatic/acid hydrolysis of starch (corn, potato, rice). CAS 9050-36-6. Dextrose Equivalent (DE) = measure of reducing power: starch = 0, dextrose = 100; DE 10-12 means ~10-12% reducing-sugar equivalence, i.e. relatively low free reducing-end content but NOT zero. Avg degree of polymerization roughly DP ~8-10. Synonyms: Dextrin 10, Maltodextrin NF, glucose polymer, starch hydrolysate. (ChemicalBook CAS 9050-36-6, Tier 2; Wikipedia/EU customs nomenclature classifies DE<10 as "dextrins", Tier 2/3). Intended use in current Formula.xlsx: S2 EnergyA at 8 g per 100 mL concentrate = 80 mg/mL concentrate, diluted 1:3 at use = ~20 mg/mL (~2% w/v) delivered, as a slow-release glucose/maltose carbohydrate carrier alongside corn syrup + D-ribose.

## 2. Three-rule findings
- **R1 solubility:** Aqueous solubility very high: "very soluble, up to 240 g in 100 g water" = ~2400 mg/mL at room temp; ~99% soluble in water at room temperature; DE 10 grade is "readily dispersible and completely soluble in water" (ScienceDirect maltodextrin overview / Wikipedia, Tier 2; tandfonline 10.1080/10942910802181024 morphological/physicochemical characterization of commercial maltodextrins by DE, Tier 1). Cold-water dispersible (potato-based grades explicitly cold-soluble); dissolution time decreases as DE rises, so DE 10-12 dissolves moderately fast (tandfonline DE characterization, Tier 1). Intended in-solution conc ~20 mg/mL delivered (80 mg/mL concentrate). Headroom = 2400 / 80 (concentrate) = ~30x, or 2400/20 = ~120x delivered. >>3 -> OK. No solubility constraint at any point in this formula. Note: at high DE-source loads, viscosity rises but DE 10-12 gives relatively low viscosity in solution; not a producibility limit here.
- **R2 stability:** Maltodextrin is a glycosidic polymer and is intrinsically stable in water: thermal, acid, and freeze stable (ScienceDirect maltodextrin overview, Tier 2). Glycosidic-bond hydrolysis is negligible at the formula pH ~5.8-6.2; meaningful hydrolysis only occurs at strongly acidic pH < 3 (branched maltodextrin study shows only "slight hydrolysis at very acidic pH < 3" lowering MW; Tier 2). So no hydrolytic loss expected at formula pH. NOT photolabile, NOT redox-sensitive itself. PRIMARY stability liability is MAILLARD/non-enzymatic browning: maltodextrin carries reducing carbonyl (reducing-sugar) ends and "can act as a reducing sugar and participate in the Maillard reaction with proteins/peptides/amino compounds" and "under certain pH and temperature conditions may undergo Maillard reactions with amino acids to produce yellowing/browning" (ScienceDirect/Maillard topic, Tier 2; US4144357A patent notes starch hydrolysates DE 0-24 retard but do not eliminate Maillard, Tier 3). Reducing power is far lower than glucose/ribose per gram (DE 10-12 vs 100 for dextrose, vs pentose ribose which is most reactive), so it is a SECONDARY Maillard contributor relative to the corn syrup + D-ribose + Na-ascorbate already in S2. 20% solution pH ~4-4.7 (DE10 spec, Tier 2). Hygroscopic but LOW hygroscopicity vs polydextrose; keep desiccated dry. Microbial: an unpreserved maltodextrin sugar solution supports microbial growth (US4596602A "microbial stability" patent, Tier 3) -> needs preservation OR dry/make-fresh. Realistic shelf-life in the wet S2 matrix is limited not by maltodextrin hydrolysis but by browning + microbial risk + sorbate failing at pH 6. Dry powder shelf-life ~18-24 mo cool/dry/desiccated (Tier 3 trade sources).
- **R3 conflicts:** PAIR -> MECHANISM -> MITIGATION. (1) Maltodextrin + reducing-sugar partners (Korean corn syrup, D-ribose) + Na-ascorbate, all in S2 -> Maillard/browning amine-independent caramelization plus reducing-sugar pool enlargement; ribose (pentose) and corn-syrup glucose are far more reactive, maltodextrin adds incrementally to the reducing-end pool and total carbohydrate browning substrate, accelerated by warmth/light. Mitigation: in winning dry-first redesign, S2 is CUT entirely; replace carbohydrate role with clean dextrose ORS, drop ribose/corn syrup; if any glucose-polymer carrier is wanted keep it DRY and away from amines/ascorbate. (2) Maltodextrin + amino acids/amines present in concentrate when capsules co-administered or if amino actives co-formulated (glutamine, taurine, citrulline, betaine, B6/P5P amine) -> classic amino-carbonyl Maillard on storage in a wet acidic-neutral warm matrix. Mitigation: keep carbohydrate carrier physically separate from amino actives (dry preload vs drink), never co-store wet. (3) Maltodextrin + osmolality target: as a polymer it contributes far LESS osmotic load per gram than mono/disaccharides (50 g/L DE11-14 < osm of 20 g/L glucose; Roquette/PMC4940893, Tier 1/2) -> this is FAVORABLE, lets you raise delivered carbohydrate without breaching the <300-320 mOsm/kg hydration ceiling. No chelation/precipitation with Ca/Mg/Zn/Fe/citrate/phosphate (inert polysaccharide; no carboxyl/phosphate donors). No redox conflict. No acid-base/DEB contribution (non-ionic).

## 3. Avian/poultry evidence
NO DIRECT AVIAN ERGOGENIC/REHYDRATION EFFICACY DATA found for maltodextrin DE10-12 as a between-round performance carbohydrate in fighting/exercising poultry. Maltodextrin is, however, a routine digestible glucose-source carbohydrate; in birds it is hydrolyzed by intestinal alpha-glucosidases (maltase) to glucose then absorbed via SGLT1, the same pathway central to this formula's hydration logic (Shibata 2023 chicken jejunal SGLT1, cited in formula rationale; Tier 1). Versus free glucose it gives lower osmotic load per gram of delivered glucose (PMC4940893 digestible-maltodextrin review; PMC2486870 reduced-osmolarity maltodextrin ORS in humans, Tier 1) — a generic GI/osmotic advantage, not avian-specific. No avian requirement and no avian max-tolerable exist because it is a caloric carbohydrate, not a nutrient with a defined requirement; tolerance is effectively limited only by osmotic/GI load and total energy, not toxicity. Acute vs chronic: relevant only acutely as an energy/osmotic carrier; no chronic poultry tolerance study located. Mark: NO AVIAN-SPECIFIC EFFICACY DATA (mechanistic plausibility via maltase->SGLT1 only).

## 4. Verdict
SKIP in the revised design (do NOT carry maltodextrin into either dry SKU). Rationale: in the winning dry-first redesign, S2 EnergyA is cut entirely; the fight-day drink is built on clean dextrose for direct SGLT1 glucose+Na cotransport, and adding a glucose POLYMER provides no acute hydration advantage over the dextrose already chosen (it must first be hydrolyzed by maltase), while reintroducing a reducing-sugar Maillard substrate that the redesign specifically removed. R1 solubility passes easily (OK, ~30-120x headroom) and R2 hydrolytic stability is fine at formula pH, so maltodextrin is not itself a BLOCK — but it is REDUNDANT and adds browning-substrate risk. CONDITIONAL exception: if a glucose-polymer is ever desired to raise delivered carbohydrate energy WITHOUT raising osmolality (its one genuine edge — lower osm per gram), it should go in the DRY fight-day stick at a modest level (e.g. swap part of dextrose for DE10-12), kept dry and away from ascorbate/amino actives, and never in a wet stored liquid. Net for current formula: SKIP / DELETE with S2.

## 5. Cautions
Welfare/safety/producibility notes (non-blocking): (1) Maltodextrin is a high-glycemic, rapidly digested glucose source (glycemic index can exceed glucose); not a welfare hazard at the small acute doses here but it is empty energy with no recovery mechanism of its own. (2) Its reducing-end carbonyls make it a real (if secondary) Maillard browning contributor in any warm, amine-containing, wet matrix — the exact failure mode of S2; do not re-create a wet sugar+amine SKU. (3) Unpreserved maltodextrin solutions support microbial growth — only acceptable in dry or acidified make-fresh form. (4) Hygroscopic: dry blends need desiccant/foil. (5) Its osmolality advantage (low osm per gram) is the only reason to ever prefer it over dextrose; if that edge is not needed, dextrose is simpler and more directly SGLT1-active. No avian toxicity concern at formulation doses.

## 9. Blocking gaps / UNKNOWN
- No avian-specific efficacy or tolerance study for maltodextrin as an exercise/between-round carbohydrate in poultry — efficacy claim rests on generic mammalian/human ORS data + maltase->SGLT1 mechanistic plausibility (Shibata 2023). Treat performance benefit as UNKNOWN in birds.
- No primary monograph located giving exact quantitative reducing-sugar (mg reducing equivalent per g) for the specific DE 10-12 grade vs dextrose under formula pH/temp — Maillard-contribution ranking vs ribose/corn-syrup is qualitative (Tier 2/3), so the quantitative browning rate in the S2 matrix is UNKNOWN.
- Aqueous shelf-life of maltodextrin specifically in the S2 multi-component matrix (with ascorbate + ribose + corn syrup at pH ~6) not directly measured — inferred BLOCK-level instability is attributed to the matrix (Maillard + sorbate failure at pH 6 + microbial), not to maltodextrin's own glycosidic hydrolysis.
