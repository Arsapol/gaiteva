# Substance Dossier: Magnesium malate

## 1. Identity
Magnesium malate = magnesium salt of malic acid (2-hydroxybutanedioic acid). Anhydrous formula C4H4MgO5, MW 156.38 g/mol, CAS 869-06-7 (PubChem CID 164748). Trihydrate (commercial supplement form) CAS 6150-86-3 (Jost Chemical product). Also marketed as 'dimagnesium malate'. ~15% elemental Mg by mass (1000 mg salt ~= 150 mg elemental Mg). Malate2- is the conjugate base of malic acid (diprotic; pKa1 ~3.40, pKa2 ~5.11). Synonyms: magnesium DL-malate, magnesium 2-hydroxybutanedioate.

## 2. Three-rule findings
- **R1 solubility:** Freely water-soluble. Commercial/datasheet figures report >50 g/100 mL (>500 mg/mL) at 25C for the trihydrate [Tier 2/3, supplier datasheets]. Even taking a conservative lower-bound of ~50 mg/mL, intended in-solution concentrations in the CURRENT formula are: S2 1.2 g/100 mL = 12 mg/mL concentrate (~3 mg/mL after 1:3 use-dilution); S5 2.4 g/200 mL = 12 mg/mL (~3 mg/mL diluted); S3 0.4 g/120 mL = 3.3 mg/mL (~0.8 mg/mL diluted). Headroom vs intended conc = solubility/conc >> 3 in every case (>=4x even at the conservative 50 mg/mL bound; >40x at the datasheet value). VERDICT R1: OK. Solubility is not a constraint. NOTE: exact pharmacopeial solubility monograph not located (UNKNOWN as Tier-1 number) — figure rests on Tier-2/3 supplier data.
- **R2 stability:** Magnesium malate is an ionic salt of a non-reducing dicarboxylic acid; in water it dissociates to Mg2+ + malate2-. No hydrolysis-labile bond, no thiol, no photolabile chromophore -> intrinsically stable in solution (unlike B12/ascorbate/NAC/P5P). Malate is NOT a reducing sugar, so it does NOT itself drive Maillard browning (contrast D-ribose/corn syrup). Stable pH window: malate buffers around pKa2 ~5.11, so it is chemically content at the formula pH ~5.8-6.2; no precipitation of the salt at this pH. Main stability concerns are systemic to the wet liquids, not the salt: (a) it adds divalent Mg2+ that can secondarily interact with other actives (see R3); (b) in a sugar-rich, near-neutral matrix the solution as a whole remains microbially vulnerable and K-sorbate is only ~6% active at pH 6 (sorbic acid pKa ~4.76) — a whole-formula defect, not specific to Mg malate. Realistic shelf-life of the salt itself in a clean acidified solution: long (months); but in the actual S2/S5 reducing-sugar matrices the limiting factor is the matrix, not the malate. Storage: trihydrate is mildly hygroscopic dry; keep desiccated. VERDICT R2: salt itself STABLE in solution; no degradation pathway intrinsic to Mg malate. Cited: malic acid pKa values [Tier 2, CRC/standard physchem]; sorbic acid pKa 4.76 [Tier 2].
- **R3 conflicts:** PAIR 1 — Mg2+ + citrate (Na/K citrate present in S2 2g Na-cit+2g K-cit, S3, S4, S5): mechanism = citrate is a strong tridentate chelator of divalent cations (Mg-citrate stability log K ~3.4), so added Mg2+ partitions into soluble Mg-citrate complexes. This is NOT a precipitation/incompatibility at these dilute concentrations (Mg-citrate is soluble) but it (i) lowers free ionic Mg2+ available and (ii) consumes citrate buffer capacity. Mitigation: not a producibility blocker; relevant only to bioavailability accounting. [Tier 1/2: citrate-Mg dissociation constants, J Phys Chem ion-association; Davies & Hoyle].
PAIR 2 — Mg2+ + glycerophosphate (Mg-glycerophosphate is in capsules, not the same liquid; no direct liquid co-formulation) -> no in-liquid conflict in current SKUs.
PAIR 3 — malate2- as an ALKALI load (acid-base/DEB): malate is a metabolizable organic anion; like citrate/lactate/acetate it is oxidized in the Krebs cycle, net-consuming protons and yielding bicarbonate -> ALKALINIZING, raising strong-ion difference [Tier 1/2: Critical Care 2025 PMC12750785 'Buffers in IV solutions']. Each malate2- carries 2 negative charges -> contributes to the heavy cumulative citrate+malate alkali load flagged as a formula-level problem (over-alkalinizes vs the exerted bird's panting respiratory alkalosis). Mitigation: this is the key reason the winning design DELETES Mg malate from the drink and moves Mg to dry feed (Mg ceiling is enteric, not blood; no alkali anion loaded into the acute drink).
PAIR 4 — Mg2+ + ascorbate (S2 Na-ascorbate 6g): Mg2+ is redox-inactive (unlike Fe/Cu), so it does NOT catalyze ascorbate oxidation. No redox conflict. Net: no precipitation/incompatibility blocker; the genuine issues are the alkalinizing malate anion and citrate sequestration of Mg.

## 3. Avian/poultry evidence
Magnesium requirement (poultry, NRC Nutrient Requirements of Poultry 1994): chicks/broilers ~600 mg/kg diet; layers similar [Tier 1, NRC]. Mg ceiling is ENTERIC, not a blood-Mg neuromuscular limit: dietary Mg above ~0.3-0.5% feed (~3000-5000 mg/kg) causes osmotic diarrhea/wet litter [Tier 1/2, NRC Mineral Tolerance of Animals 2005, magnesium chapter]. No avian study specifically on magnesium MALATE as the salt form was located -> the malate counter-ion's avian effect (metabolizable alkalinizing anion) is inferred from general organic-anion acid-base physiology, NOT from a poultry Mg-malate trial. NO AVIAN DATA specific to magnesium malate efficacy/tolerance as a distinct salt. Acute drink Mg in current formula: S5 Mg total ~619 mg (malate + MgCl2) is high for a single between-rounds session vs a 2.5 kg bird; better delivered chronically in feed where the enteric ceiling governs. Birds are uricotelic -> renal excess-Mg clearance is efficient, but acute gut osmotic effect (loose droppings) is the practical limiter.

## 4. Verdict
SKIP magnesium malate as a distinct ingredient / BLOCK its use in the fight-day drink. Aligns with winning design: CUT Mg malate from S2/S3/S5 (all liquids deleted). Reasons: (1) the malate counter-ion is an alkalinizing organic anion that compounds the over-alkalinizing citrate load and fights the bird's panting respiratory alkalosis; (2) divalent Mg in the acute drink is the wrong vehicle — Mg's relevant ceiling is enteric, so Mg belongs in DRY FEED PRELOAD, not the SGLT1 ORS (drink Mg target ~0). R1 solubility is OK and R2 salt-stability is fine, so this is NOT a chemistry block — it is a physiology/design SKIP. If magnesium supplementation is wanted, deliver elemental Mg via feed premix (Mg malate acceptable there as a dry, ~15% elemental, non-Maillard source), dosed below the ~0.3-0.5% feed enteric ceiling. Dose recommendation: 0 mg/L in fight-day drink; if used dry in preload, keep total feed Mg under enteric ceiling (~150 mg elemental/bird/day region reasonable, monitor droppings).

## 5. Cautions
1) WELFARE/SAFETY (non-blocking): high acute Mg in the drink (S5 ~619 mg session) risks osmotic loose droppings / transient gut upset in a 2.5 kg bird; enteric, dose-dependent, reversible. Move Mg to feed to avoid. 2) The malate anion adds to cumulative alkali — sum malate + all citrate across any co-given SKUs when judging DEB; near-neutral Cl-balanced is the avian-evidenced target. 3) Supplier solubility/pH figures are Tier 2/3; treat as approximate. 4) Mg malate is mildly hygroscopic — desiccate in dry preload. 5) Citrate co-presence sequesters free Mg2+ (bioavailability note, not a safety hazard). 6) 'Dimagnesium malate' vs mono-magnesium malate stoichiometry differs — confirm elemental Mg per label, do not assume.

## 8. Peer review
workflow adversarial-verify lane 2026-06-16: **CONFIRMED** (confidence high). Corrections: MECHANISM IMPRECISION (R3 Pair 3, load-bearing citation): The draft says malate is 'oxidized in the Krebs cycle, net-consuming protons and yielding bicarbonate -> ALKALINIZING, raising strong-ion difference' and cites PMC12750785. The cited paper's actual central thesis is the OPPOSITE framing: it argues the alkalinizing effect is NOT from bicarbonate generation per se (Krebs-cycle metabolization yields bicarbonate together with an H+, so it does not cause alkalosis by itself), but rather from the accompanying CATION load raising the plasma strong-ion difference (SID). The draft's DIRECTION (alkalinizing, raises SID) is correct and the cut-from-drink conclusion is unaffected, but the stated mechanism ('yielding bicarbonate -> alkalinizing') is exactly the misconception the paper refutes. Reword to: 'removal of the malate anion via metabolism raises SID; the alkalinizing effect tracks the accompanying cation load, not bicarbonate generation.'; CATION NUANCE: For Mg malate specifically, the SID-raising cation is Mg2+ (divalent, and partly sequestered by co-present citrate per the draft's own Pair 1). Attributing the alkali load purely to 'the malate anion' slightly overstates malate's independent contribution vs e.g. Na citrate where Na+ is the SID driver. Minor; does not change the SKIP verdict.; R1 SOLUBILITY OVERSTATED IN SUPPLIER TIER: Datasheet/SDS language located is weaker than the dossier's '>50 g/100 mL' headline -- Anmol SDS and others say only 'partially soluble in cold water'/'soluble', and PubChem lists no quantitative aqueous solubility. Not decision-blocking.; pKa2 minor spread: draft cites malic acid pKa2 ~5.11; corroborating sources give 5.03 (ECHA, 20C) to 5.20 (Wikipedia).
