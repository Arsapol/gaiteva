# Substance Dossier: L-Carnitine L-tartrate (LCLT)

## 1. Identity
L-Carnitine L-tartrate (LCLT), CAS 36687-82-8. A 2:1 salt of L-carnitine cation with L-(+)-tartaric acid; molecular formula C18H36N2O12, MW 472.49 g/mol; ~68 wt% L-carnitine (free L-carnitine MW 161.2; tartrate dianion shared 2:1). Synonyms: L-carnitine tartrate, LCLT, Carnipure Tartrate (Lonza tradename). Free L-carnitine = (R)-3-hydroxy-4-(trimethylammonio)butanoate, CAS 541-15-1. The tartrate salt is the standard non-hygroscopic-grade, free-flowing, tabletting/solution form of carnitine (free carnitine inner salt is intensely hygroscopic and hard to handle). In CURRENT formula: S3 Reset B, 4 g LCLT / 120 mL concentrate = 33.3 g/L concentrate -> ~2.72 g/L free-carnitine equivalent; at 1:3 use-dilution ~8.3 g/L LCLT (~5.6 g/L carnitine). Source: ChemicalBook CB5141230 (Tier 2); Sigma-Aldrich product monograph (Tier 2).

## 2. Three-rule findings
- **R1 solubility:** Intended in-solution conc: 33.3 g/L LCLT in S3 concentrate (8.3 g/L at 1:3 use). LCLT is FREELY/very soluble in water: >1 g/mL (>1000 g/L) at 20 C per supplier/patent monographs (US5952379 non-hygroscopic carnitine-salt patent; ChemicalBook CB5141230, Tier 2). Free L-carnitine itself is extremely water-soluble (~2.5 g/mL). Headroom vs 33.3 g/L concentrate = >1000/33.3 = >30x; vs 8.3 g/L use = >120x. VERDICT R1 = OK (massive headroom; solubility is a non-issue at any concentration in this formula). No salt-form solubility limitation. Note: high aqueous solubility is coupled to high hygroscopicity of the dry powder (manufacturing caution, not a solution caution) — Hrcak file 274794 "Solving manufacturing problems for L-carnitine-L-tartrate" (Tier 2).
- **R2 stability:** LCLT is one of the MORE stable actives in this formula in water. Monograph data: stable in aqueous solution at pH 3-6 for >1 year and withstands >200 C (ChemicalBook L-carnitine article, Tier 2). Formula S3 pH is ~5.8-6.2 (top of, but within, the stable window) and S3 is explicitly acidified (citric acid 0.4 g + citrate buffer). No hydrolysis-labile bond in the carnitine zwitterion under these conditions; tartrate counter-ion is also hydrolytically stable. NOT a major oxidation target (no thiol/ene-diol; unlike NAC/ascorbate/P5P/B12 in the same formula). Not notably photolabile. Carnitine has a free secondary -OH and a carboxylate but is not a reducing sugar; the quaternary trimethylammonium N is non-nucleophilic, so LCLT adds negligibly to the S2 reducing-sugar Maillard stack (contrast primary-amine amino acids). Realistic shelf-life in a pH ~6 aqueous concentrate: carnitine moiety stable >12 mo; the BINDING wet-product shelf-life constraint for S3 is microbial (K-sorbate only ~6% active at pH 6; sorbic acid pKa 4.76) and the co-formulated labile actives (methyl-B12 photolysis ~pH 5, P5P), NOT carnitine. VERDICT R2 = stable/OK as a molecule; dry-preload home strictly superior (removes hygroscopic-caking concern). Sources: ChemicalBook (Tier 2); sorbic-acid pKa Tier 1 physchem.
- **R3 conflicts:** Pair-by-pair vs S3 Reset B and the wider formula: (1) LCLT-tartrate + divalent metals (Mg malate, MgCl2 in S5/F5; trace Ca): CALCIUM TARTRATE is poorly soluble (~20x less soluble than potassium bitartrate; Wikipedia Calcium tartrate, Tier 2) -> if appreciable Ca were present, tartrate could precipitate. This formula has NO significant Ca source -> low risk. MAGNESIUM tartrate is classed water-soluble (patent listing of soluble Mg salts, Tier 2/3) -> Mg + LCLT tartrate does NOT precipitate at these concs. Mitigation: keep Ca sources out of any LCLT solution. (2) LCLT + citrate/citric acid (S3): no conflict; both hold pH in carnitine's stable 3-6 window; tartrate + citrate both weak-acid anions, no precipitation. (3) Carnitine quaternary amine + reducing sugars (S2/S5 corn syrup/maltodextrin/ribose): non-nucleophilic N -> negligible Maillard. (4) Tartrate adds minor acid-anion load (metabolizable; small vs citrate load). (5) No redox antagonism with NAC/ascorbate (carnitine redox-inert). VERDICT R3 = compatible in THIS formula (no Ca); only real precip lever is Ca-tartrate, which is absent. Sources: Wikipedia Calcium tartrate (Tier 2); Mg-tartrate solubility patent listing (Tier 2/3).

## 3. Avian/poultry evidence
STRONG poultry evidence — carnitine is mechanism-relevant and welfare-positive in birds, but as a CHRONIC FED additive, not an acute drink lever. Poultry effective dose ~50-300 mg/kg feed; growth/BW benefit mainly 300-800 mg/kg, abdominal-fat reduction 50-900 mg/kg, lipid lowering 100-600 mg/kg (Hellenic Vet Med Soc review, ekt.gr, Tier 2). Cardio/ascites/pulmonary-hypertension protection: 100 mg/kg feed raised plasma NO, lowered MDA, reduced right-ventricular hypertrophy and ascites mortality (Khajali, J Poult Sci 50(2) 2013 jstage 50_0120145; J Poult Sci 52(3) 2015; Tier 1/2). Cold-stress/high-altitude: improved antioxidant status, lipid profile, pulmonary hypertension (BMC Vet Res 2025, DOI 10.1186/s12917-025-04813-w, Tier 1; PMC12434236, Tier 1). Heat-stress: 200 mg/kg improved antioxidant status/HSP70 (Anim Nutr, ScienceDirect S1056617123000466, Tier 1). Growth with animal fat: PMID 27047119 / PMC4774796 (Tier 1). Mechanism: mitochondrial long-chain fatty-acid beta-oxidation (CPT carnitine shuttle) -> chronic cardiac/skeletal energetics + antioxidant tone. ACUTE caveat (decisive here): muscle carnitine uptake is SLOW/refractory to acute dosing; oral bioavailability 10-20%, rapid renal clearance, single doses do not raise muscle pool (NIH ODS Carnitine fact sheet, Tier 1; AJCN supplemental-carnitine review S0002916523067448, Tier 2). So the S3 between-rounds sip does little for muscle carnitine — benefit needs days of fed loading. Max tolerable: well tolerated to >=800-1000 mg/kg feed in broilers, no toxicity reported (review, Tier 2); renally cleared (consistent with uricotelic excretion of water-soluble excess). No avian neuro/cardiotoxic ceiling at feed doses.

## 4. Verdict
ADD-conditional, RELOCATE: move L-carnitine to the DRY PRELOAD (feed); DELETE L-Carnitine L-tartrate from the acute fight-day drink and all liquids. Rationale: (a) muscle carnitine uptake is slow/refractory to acute dosing (NIH ODS; AJCN review) -> the S3 acute sip is near-useless for the muscle pool; (b) all documented avian benefits (ascites/pulmonary-hypertension protection, antioxidant tone, cardiac energetics, fat metabolism) are CHRONIC FED effects (Khajali 2013/2015; BMC Vet Res 2025); (c) dry feed avoids hygroscopic caking and any Ca-tartrate precip risk. RECOMMENDED PRELOAD DOSE: L-carnitine 100-150 mg/kg feed (~12-18 mg free-carnitine/bird/day at 120-150 g intake); deliver via L-carnitine L-tartrate ~150-220 mg/kg feed (68% carnitine). For the cardio/ascites endpoint, 100 mg/kg feed is the evidenced sweet spot (Khajali). It is OPTIONAL-but-defensible (clear avian cardio/ascites data) — rank above D-ribose/beta-alanine/Rhodiola, below creatine/betaine/NAC. SKIP in the fight-day drink and any revised liquid SKU.

## 5. Cautions
WELFARE/SAFETY (non-blocking notes): (1) Benefit is chronic and welfare-positive (lower ascites mortality, better cardiac/antioxidant status under heat/cold/hypoxia) — appropriate for the bird's own health; supports dual-use recovery framing. (2) DOSE-RESPONSE NOT MONOTONIC: one water-route trial found higher mortality at 200-250 mg/L vs 100 mg/L — more is not better; stay near 100 mg/kg feed for the cardio endpoint. (3) Tartrate counter-ion is a mild GI laxative/irritant in excess (echemi/Codeage, Tier 3) — negligible at feed doses, another reason not to load it acutely. (4) Manufacturing: LCLT dry powder is hygroscopic and adheres to punches (Hrcak 274794) -> package dry premix with desiccant, low-humidity blend; add with other moisture-sensitive actives (NAC). (5) Off-feed birds get no loading (timing risk, same as creatine/betaine). (6) Acute-sip inefficacy is the main reason to delete from S3 — keeping it wastes a labeled active and gives false assurance. (7) High carnitine -> TMA/TMAO via gut microbes in mammals; avian relevance low at these doses, not a welfare blocker, noted.

## 8. Peer review
workflow adversarial-verify lane 2026-06-16: **CONFIRMED** (confidence high). Corrections: MINOR (identity): ChemicalBook lists ~57 wt% L-carnitine for the tartrate salt, NOT 68%. The dossier's '~68 wt%' appears to be the inverse of the molar arithmetic done wrong: 2x161.2 / 472.49 = 322.4/472.49 = 68.2%. That ratio is correct only if the salt were carnitine2-tartrate with TWO carnitine cations per ONE tartrate dianion. The real stoichiometry IS 2 carnitine : 1 tartrate (C18H36N2O12 = 2x C7H15NO3 carnitine + C4H6O6 tartaric acid; 2x161.2 + 150.09 = 472.49), so 322.4/472.49 = 68.2% IS the carnitine-base fraction. Supplier 'Carnipure'/Lonza datasheets quote ~58-68% depending on whether free-base or inner-salt basis; the 68% figure is internally consistent with the stated formula and MW and is defensible. NET: no correction required, but flag that some datasheets cite ~58% (inner-salt basis) and the downstream free-carnitine-equivalent dose math (2.72 g/L) carries that 10pt uncertainty band. Non-blocking.; MINOR (R3): dossier says calcium tartrate '~20x less soluble than potassium bitartrate'; independent check gives Ca-tartrate ~0.53 g/L at 20C (Wikipedia, confirmed) vs K-bitartrate ~5.7 g/L at 20C, i.e. ~11x not 20x. Direction and conclusion (Ca-tartrate is the only real precipitation lever, and Ca is absent from the formula) are CORRECT; the multiplier is overstated. Non-blocking.; CLARIFY (avian dose): Khajali 2013 tested 50/100/150 mg/kg as L-CARNITINE (the additive level), published J Poult Sci 50(2):143-149. Dossier citation is accurate; just note the 100 mg/kg sweet-spot is carnitine-base, so LCLT delivery at 68% = ~147 mg LCLT/kg feed (dossier's 150-220 mg/kg LCLT range brackets this correctly).

## 9. Blocking gaps / UNKNOWN
- No primary pharmacopeial monograph (USP/EP) located for exact aqueous solubility number at 25 C — relied on supplier/ChemicalBook >1 g/mL figure (Tier 2); UNKNOWN: pharmacopeial mg/mL value (non-blocking, headroom is enormous regardless).
- No avian-specific study of L-carnitine delivered in DRINKING WATER between exercise bouts (all positive avian data are fed/chronic); acute water-route gamefowl efficacy is UNKNOWN — supports the SKIP-in-drink verdict rather than blocking it.
- Exact magnesium tartrate solubility constant (g/L, Ksp) not found in a Tier 1 source — classed 'water-soluble' only (Tier 2/3); non-blocking because formula Mg is recommended moved to feed and no Ca is present.
- No gamefowl-specific carnitine dose study; feed-dose recommendation extrapolated from broiler cardio/ascites/heat-stress trials (Tier 1/2) — reasonable but UNKNOWN for fighting-breed adults specifically.
