# Substance Dossier: Sodium citrate (trisodium citrate dihydrate; alkalinizing / DEB agent)

## 1. Identity
Sodium citrate = trisodium citrate (the alkalinizing salt of citric acid). Predominant commercial/food form is trisodium citrate dihydrate, Na3C6H5O7·2H2O, MW 294.10 g/mol, CAS 6132-04-3 (anhydrous trisodium citrate CAS 68-04-2, MW 258.07). Sodium content ~23.5% w/w of the dihydrate (3 Na = 68.97 / 294.10). Also exists as monosodium and disodium citrate (acidic/buffer forms) but the formula uses the tribasic "sodium citrate dihydrate" alkali. Synonyms: citric acid trisodium salt dihydrate, sodium citrate tribasic dihydrate, E331(iii). In this formula it appears in S2 EnergyA 5 g/100 mL, S3 Reset B 2 g/120 mL, S4 Oxygen 4 g/250 mL, S5 ATP Booster 3.2 g/200 mL, F5 ATP Express 2.8 g/150 mL — paired in every product with K-citrate and (in S3/S5/F5) a small citric-acid back-titration, functioning as the Na source + bicarbonate-precursor alkali/DEB lever. [Tier 2 ChemicalBook 6132-04-3; Tier 2 Sigma-Aldrich substance 294-10-6132-04-3; Wikipedia Trisodium citrate]

## 2. Three-rule findings
- **R1 solubility:** Trisodium citrate dihydrate aqueous solubility ~650 g/L (65 g/100 mL) at 25C; 1 g dissolves in ~1.5 mL water (~667 g/L); some sources up to ~720 g/L [Tier 2 ChemicalBook CB9752065; ICSC 1219; ACS Reagent Chemicals monograph]. Intended in-solution concentration: highest is S2 at 5 g/100 mL = 50 g/L concentrate (~12.5 g/L at the 1:3 use dilution); other products 16.7-26.7 g/L concentrate. Headroom in the concentrate = 650 / 50 ≈ 13x; at use dilution ≈ 52x. R1 = OK with very large headroom — solubility is NOT a limiting factor for sodium citrate at any concentration used here, even co-dissolved with the other freely soluble citrate/electrolyte salts. (In the WINNING dry-stick design, sodium citrate dihydrate is retained only as a minimal 0.3 g/L buffer — even more trivially soluble.) Verdict R1: OK.
- **R2 stability:** Sodium citrate is chemically very stable in aqueous solution: it does not hydrolyze, oxidize, or photodegrade and has no reducing-sugar/Maillard reactivity itself; loses crystal water ~150C, decomposes only at high temperature [Tier 2 ChemicalBook CB9752065; ScienceDirect "Trisodium Citrate - an overview"]. The active itself is essentially shelf-stable in water indefinitely (microbial growth aside). The STABILITY PROBLEM it creates is indirect and pH-mediated: a 5% trisodium-citrate solution is alkaline, pH ~7.5-9.0, because citrate sequentially deprotonates/hydrolyzes water [Tier 2 ChemicalBook CB9752065]. Citric acid pKa1 2.92 / pKa2 4.28 / pKa3 5.21 [Tier 2 ChemicalBook; Sigma Buffer Reference Center]. The formula back-titrates with citric acid + K-sorbate to hold pH 5.8-6.2, but the large trisodium-citrate load is the dominant base that pushes pH up into the zone where (a) K-sorbate is only ~6% undissociated active (sorbic acid pKa ~4.76) and worse after 1:3 dilution, and (b) P5P/B12/ascorbate degrade faster. So sodium citrate is stability-INERT itself but is the main agent raising formula pH and thereby crippling the only preservative and accelerating loss of the labile actives. Realistic shelf-life of the wet products is therefore preservative/pH-limited, not citrate-limited. Storage: dry salt is non-hygroscopic-stable; keep sealed. Verdict R2: the substance is stable, but it is a pH-driver that undermines preservation — TIGHT in the wet formula, fully resolved by the dry design.
- **R3 conflicts:** (1) Sodium citrate + divalent cations (Mg malate/MgCl2 in S2/S3/S4/S5/F5; any Ca/Zn/Fe): citrate is a chelator/sequestrant and forms soluble Mg-citrate/Ca-citrate complexes -> mechanism: citrate ties up free Mg2+/Ca2+, lowering free divalent activity; at the high citrate concentrations here excess citrate keeps Ca/Mg-citrate SOLUBLE (CaCit- soluble complex) rather than precipitating [Tier 1 J Phys Chem ion-association citrate Na/K/Ca/Mg complexes DOI 10.1021/j100819a045; Tier 2 ScienceDirect calcium-citrate]. Mitigation: in the wet formula no hard precipitate expected (citrate excess solubilizes), but it reduces ionic free-Mg bioavailability; in the dry redesign Mg is moved to feed and kept OUT of the citrate-containing drink — clean separation. (2) Sodium citrate + the heavy K-citrate/Na-citrate alkali stack -> physiological acid-base conflict (this is the headline conflict, not a precipitation one): trisodium citrate is an alkali load. Mechanism: each citrate is oxidized in the TCA cycle to yield 3 bicarbonate, AND the co-administered Na+ raises plasma strong-ion-difference (SID); the SID/Na mechanism (not just bicarbonate) drives plasma alkalinization, maximal with TRIsodium citrate [Tier 1 PMC8128256 sodium-citrate alkalosis via SID; Tier 1 PMC6787309; Tier 1 Hering-Smith Ann Transl Med citrate->3 HCO3-]. The exerted bird has a MIXED disturbance (panting respiratory alkalosis + lactate metabolic acidosis); piling on citrate alkali risks alkalosis overshoot and is not avian-evidenced as beneficial acutely. Mitigation: cut total citrate dramatically; use only a minimal Na-citrate buffer (~0.3 g/L) balanced with Cl (NaCl/KCl) to land near-neutral DEB; this is exactly the WINNING fight-day-drink approach (DEB +3 mEq/L, Cl ~64 mM). (3) Sodium citrate + K-sorbate -> preservative antagonism via pH (see R2): citrate alkalinity raises pH above sorbate's effective window. Mitigation: dry/make-fresh design removes the need for sorbate entirely. No direct adverse reaction with creatine, glutamine, NAC, taurine, DMG, citrulline (compatible).

## 3. Avian/poultry evidence
Sodium is an essential macromineral and the principal cation of the avian DEB (Na+K-Cl) lever; sodium citrate is a recognized Na source AND a bicarbonate-precursor alkali. POULTRY DEB evidence: a DEB of ~50-250 mEq/kg diet promotes acid-base normalization and good performance in broilers, with ~110 mEq/kg optimal for young birds; raising DEB increases water intake, lowers rectal temperature, and aids heat-stress acid-base balance [Tier 1 Araujo/Borges 2022 PMID 35435521 "Effects of dietary electrolyte balance...broilers"; Tier 1 review World's Poultry Sci J electrolytes/DEB acid-base; Tier 2 WATTAgNet/FeedStrategy DEB reviews]. Sodium bicarbonate/sodium salts are the standard practical DEB raisers under heat stress. NO POULTRY-SPECIFIC PHARMACOKINETIC STUDY of trisodium-CITRATE-as-acute-drink was found — the avian alkalinizing inference is mechanistic (citrate->bicarbonate + Na->SID, established in mammals: PMC8128256, Hering-Smith) extrapolated onto the poultry DEB framework. Acute vs chronic: DEB is a CHRONIC dietary construct (mEq/kg feed over days); there is no avian evidence that an acute between-rounds citrate alkali bolus improves recovery, and the avian physiology frame warns the exerted bird is already in panting respiratory ALKALOSIS, so acute alkali loading is physiologically counter-indicated -> target near-neutral Cl-balanced drink instead. Dose framing: keep session Na within ORS range (~50-55 mM Na in the drink); chronic feed DEB target ~150-300 mEq/kg [Tier 1 PMID 35435521; NRC Nutrient Requirements of Poultry 1994]. No avian toxicity ceiling for citrate per se found; sodium excess in uricotelic birds is renally cleared if water is available, but high Na drives water intake/wet litter.

## 4. Verdict
SKIP as an alkali/DEB-loading agent in the acute fight-day drink; ADD-conditional ONLY as a minimal pH buffer. The substance is solubility-safe (OK) and chemically stable (the salt itself), so it does not fail R1/R2 on its own merits — but its FUNCTIONAL role (heavy trisodium-citrate alkali across all five wet liquids) fails the avian acid-base logic (over-alkalinizes vs panting respiratory alkalosis) and is the dominant pH-driver that breaks K-sorbate preservation and accelerates B-vitamin/ascorbate loss. RECOMMENDATION matching the winning design: in the FIGHT-DAY DRINK keep sodium citrate dihydrate at only ~0.3 g/L as a minimal buffer (NOT alkali-loading), Cl-balanced with NaCl 2.9 g/L + KCl 1.1 g/L + citric acid 0.7 g/L to hold near-neutral DEB (+3 mEq/L) and pH ~3.8-4.2; this preserves a touch of buffering/palatability while abandoning the alkalinizing function. DELETE the gram-scale citrate alkali stacks in S2/S3/S4/S5/F5. Do NOT preload citrate in feed (Na/DEB is better dosed via balanced Na/K/Cl salts to a measured mEq/kg target). Net: ADD-conditional (trace buffer in fight-day drink) / SKIP (as alkali lever everywhere else).

## 5. Cautions
WELFARE/SAFETY NOTES (non-blocking): (1) Acute alkali overshoot — loading trisodium citrate on top of K-citrate into a bird already in panting respiratory alkalosis can push toward metabolic alkalosis; mitigated by switching to near-neutral Cl-balanced design. (2) Sodium load / water-litter — high dietary/drink Na increases water intake and wet droppings; on repeated daily use monitor Na intake and litter; uricotelic renal clearance handles modest excess only if water is freely available. (3) GI symptoms — in mammals gram-scale sodium citrate causes osmotic GI upset/diarrhea; analogous enteric tolerance limit plausible in birds at high concentrate doses. (4) Preservative interaction — by raising pH, citrate is the main reason K-sorbate falls below effective range in the wet products; treat any wet citrate product as under-preserved. (5) Mg bioavailability — citrate chelates the formula's Mg; co-formulating gram citrate with Mg reduces free Mg activity (functional, not a hard precipitate). None of these block the research; all are resolved by the dry, low-citrate, Cl-balanced redesign.
