# Substance: Citric acid (anhydrous; also monohydrate)

# Substance Dossier: Citric acid (anhydrous; also monohydrate)

## 1. Identity
Citric acid, 2-hydroxypropane-1,2,3-tricarboxylic acid. Anhydrous: CAS 77-92-9, C6H8O7, MW 192.12 g/mol. Monohydrate: CAS 5949-29-1, C6H8O7·H2O, MW 210.14 g/mol. Conjugate base = citrate (trisodium/tripotassium citrate are the salt buffers already in the formula). Synonyms: 2-hydroxy-1,2,3-propanetricarboxylic acid, E330. Triprotic weak organic acid. In this formula it is a free-acid acidulant: S3 Reset B 0.4 g/120 mL (~0.33%), S5 ATP Booster 0.6 g/200 mL (~0.30%), F5 ATP Express 0.5-1 g/150 mL (~0.33-0.67%), and proposed fight-day ORS stick 0.7 g/L (~0.07%). Sources: Wikipedia Citric acid (Tier 2); USDA AMS Citric Acid Technical Report 2015 (Tier 2); USP-NF/ACS monograph (Tier 1).

## 2. Three-rule findings
- **R1 solubility:** Extremely water-soluble. Anhydrous solubility ~59.2% w/w @20C (~1450 mg/mL); reported ~1174 g/L @10C and ~1809 g/L @30C (~1450-1800 mg/mL @25-30C) [Wikipedia Citric acid, Tier 2; ChemicalBook 77-92-9, Tier 2]. Intended concentrations are trivially low: highest F5 ~0.67% (~6.7 mg/mL), S3/S5 ~0.3% (~3 mg/mL), proposed ORS ~0.07% (~0.7 mg/mL). Headroom = ~1450/6.7 ≈ 216x at the most concentrated point, far above the >=3 OK threshold. Verdict: OK. No salting-out concern alongside dextrose/sugars/citrate salts at these loads.
- **R2 stability:** Hydrolytically and oxidatively STABLE in aqueous solution at ambient/refrigerated temperatures — no hydrolyzable bond, does not oxidize; it is a stabilizer (acidulant + metal chelator) that PROTECTS other actives. Thermal decomposition (aconitic/itaconic → citraconic anhydride) needs >148C, appreciable only ~180C/30 min — irrelevant to a drink [Thermal decomposition of citric acid, Thermochim. Acta, DOI 10.1016/0040-6031(86)87081-2, Tier 2]. Citric-acid solutions ≤100C show less precipitate; good stability to 18 mo at 2-8C [USPTO stability patent, Tier 3, directional]. Stable/buffering pH 2.5-7.0 via pKa1 3.13, pKa2 4.76, pKa3 6.40 (25C) [Wikipedia; buffer refs, Tier 2]. At formula pH 5.8-6.2 it is the deliberate proton donor holding pH down. KEY: by lowering pH it INCREASES K-sorbate efficacy (sorbic acid pKa 4.76 → more protonated active sorbic acid), so in legacy wet liquids citric acid helps the borderline sorbate system. Shelf-life of the citric acid component: years dry, many months refrigerated in solution; never the limiting active. Not light/O2-sensitive. As an acidulant it suppresses microbial growth (net positive). Verdict: STABLE — a stability ASSET, not a liability.
- **R3 conflicts:** Citric acid → citrate strongly chelates Ca/Mg/Zn/Fe/Cu. (1) Citrate + Mg (Mg malate/MgCl2 in S5/F5; Mg-glycerophosphate caps) → soluble Mg-citrate complexes; mechanism chelation; at ~0.3% acid + citrate-salt loads keeps Mg SOLUBLE (protective vs precipitation, though it masks free Mg2+ activity) — no precipitate, not a BLOCK. (2) Citric acid + Na/K citrate already present → intentional conjugate acid/base BUFFER; no conflict but ADDS to total citrate/alkali load — caution: citrate metabolizes to bicarbonate in vivo, feeding the over-alkalinizing DEB the redesign flags. (3) Citric acid + ascorbate/NAC (S2, caps): chelates trace Fe/Cu that catalyze ascorbate/thiol oxidation → PROTECTIVE (metal sequestration). (4) Citric acid + reducing sugars + amino acids (S2 Maillard stack): low pH modestly SLOWS Maillard (browning faster near neutral) → marginally protective, doesn't rescue S2. (5) Citric acid + sorbate (all liquids): low pH raises active sorbic-acid fraction → PROTECTIVE. Net: conflicts are overwhelmingly protective/intentional; only genuine downside is the citrate counterpart's alkalinizing metabolic load.

## 3. Avian/poultry evidence
Strong direct poultry evidence as a dietary/water acidulant (not an i.v. nutrient). Broiler review with polynomial-regression optima ~1.65-2.65% dietary citric acid: BW gain opt 2.41%, feed intake 2.22%, CP digestibility 2.65%, ME 1.65%, dressing % 2.56%; 0.5-1% improves mineral retention, villus height, immune response [Poultry Science 2024, PMID 39705837, DOI 10.1016/j.psj.2024.104668, Tier 1]. Mechanism in birds: lowers gut/crop pH → suppresses E. coli/Salmonella/coliforms, improves Ca/P solubilization & retention, raises antioxidant capacity, improves villus morphology [same review + organic-acid reviews, Tier 1/2]. Drinking water ~1.5% reported effective for growth [broiler water-acidification studies, Tier 2]. Acute vs chronic: evidence is CHRONIC (dietary over grow-out); NO avian data that the small acute acidulant dose in a fight-day drink gives ergogenic/recovery benefit — here it is a pH/taste/microbial-control aid, not a physiological active. Max tolerable/adverse (dose-dependent): 4% diet (40 g/kg) cut ADG 8.6% and intake 12.5%; 6% (60 g/kg) reduced gain, intake, bone mineral content, digestibility with acidic stress and possible liver injury [PMID 39705837, Tier 1]. The in-solution concentrations here (~0.07-0.67%; fractions of a gram per session) are far below adverse dietary thresholds.

## 4. Verdict
ADD-conditional. KEEP citric acid as a pH-adjuster/acidulant in the proposed fight-day ORS stick (~0.5-0.8 g/L, set pH ~3.8-4.2 dry-reconstituted for taste + microbial control) — highly soluble, hydrolytically stable, microbially protective, poultry-evidenced, welfare-positive, producibility asset. In legacy wet liquids (S3/S5/F5) the citric acid itself is fine; those SKUs are CUT for OTHER reasons (D-ribose, Maillard, sorbate-at-pH-6), so citric acid exits with them, not due to any citric-acid defect. Do NOT raise citrate-salt load to buffer — keep citric acid as the FREE ACID (pH-lowering) and minimize added Na/K citrate (alkalinizing DEB caution). Dose: 0.5-0.8 g/L in reconstituted ORS; no role in dry feed preload. This is a formulation-excipient verdict, not a physiological-active claim. Applies to: revised fight-day drink (keep) / legacy liquids (cut with SKU).

## 5. Cautions
1) Free-acid citric lowers in-bottle pH (good for sorbate/microbial control) but its citrate conjugate base metabolizes to bicarbonate in vivo, adding to the over-alkalinizing DEB the redesign corrects — minimize total citrate salts; use citric ACID for pH and Cl-balanced electrolytes for DEB. 2) High dietary citric acid is NOT benign at scale: ≥4% diet depresses intake/gain, ≥6% causes acidic stress, lower bone mineral content, possible liver injury (PMID 39705837); drink doses here are far below this, but do not let handlers over-acidify or stack acidulants. 3) Strong Ca/Mg/Zn/Fe chelator — keep divalent metals (Mg) OUT of the citric-acid drink; Mg belongs in the dry feed preload (consistent with redesign). 4) Identity: anhydrous (77-92-9) vs monohydrate (5949-29-1) differ ~9% by mass — specify form for accurate dosing. 5) Welfare (non-blocking): citric acid as a drink acidulant is welfare-neutral-to-positive (palatability, fresh-ORS microbial safety); no welfare concern at concentrations used.

## 9. Blocking gaps / UNKNOWN
- UNKNOWN: no avian-specific data that an acute citric-acid acidulant dose (fight-day drink) confers physiological/ergogenic/recovery benefit — its role here is purely formulation (pH/taste/microbial), so the active-benefit claim is UNKNOWN for acute use (acceptable, role is excipient).
- LOW-CONFIDENCE: exact aqueous shelf-life of the FULL reconstituted ORS (citric acid + dextrose + electrolytes + citrulline) is not directly measured in cited sources; relies on dry/make-fresh design rather than a stability study (mitigated by make-fresh-daily).
- UNKNOWN magnitude: quantitative Mg-citrate complex fraction and its effect on Mg bioavailability at these specific co-concentrations is not directly cited (general chelation only); mitigated by keeping Mg out of the drink.
