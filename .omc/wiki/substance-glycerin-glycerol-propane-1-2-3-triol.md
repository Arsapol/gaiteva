# Substance: Glycerin (glycerol; propane-1,2,3-triol)

# Substance Dossier: Glycerin (glycerol; propane-1,2,3-triol)

## 1. Identity
Glycerin / glycerol / glycerine; IUPAC propane-1,2,3-triol; C3H8O3, MW 92.09 g/mol. CAS 56-81-5. Synonyms: glycerin USP, glycerol, 1,2,3-propanetriol, sugar alcohol/triol. Pharmacopeial grade = Glycerin USP (>=99.5% / 99.7% non-GMO grades common). Clear, colorless, odorless, viscous hygroscopic liquid; specific gravity ~1.261 (20C). A trihydric alcohol (triol) — NOT a reducing sugar. Sources: Wikipedia Glycerol; USP Monograph Glycerin (pharmacopeia.cn usp29nf24); Sigma-Aldrich CAS 56-81-5 (Tier 2).

## 2. Three-rule findings
- **R1 solubility:** Glycerin is fully MISCIBLE with water in ALL proportions (infinitely soluble), owing to its three hydroxyl groups; also miscible with ethanol. Intended use here is as a co-solvent/humectant at very low volume: 2-3 mL glycerin per 100-250 mL concentrate (~1-1.5% v/v), diluted further 1:3 at use. Because glycerin is liquid and water-miscible at any ratio, there is NO saturation/solubility limit and NO precipitation risk at the intended concentration. Headroom = effectively infinite (>>3). Verdict: OK (solubility is a non-issue; it is itself a solvent). Sources: Wikipedia Glycerol (miscible with water, hygroscopic); USP Monograph Glycerin; Handbook of Pharmaceutical Excipients-type datasheet (Cargill USP Excipient Glycerin) (Tier 1/2).
- **R2 stability:** Glycerin is one of the MOST chemically stable excipients. Pure glycerin is NOT prone to atmospheric oxidation under ordinary storage; it only decomposes on HEATING with evolution of toxic acrolein (relevant >~280-350C pyrolysis, accelerated by silica/metals/salts like MgSO4, K2HPO4 — not relevant to a cool aqueous drink). Mixtures of glycerin with water, ethanol, and propylene glycol are chemically stable (Handbook of Pharmaceutical Excipients / Cargill datasheet). It is stable across the entire formula pH range (~5.8-6.2) and far wider — patent US6784326B1 notes oxidative-degradation stabilization windows of pH ~4-5.5 or >10, but ordinary storage needs no such adjustment. No hydrolysis (no hydrolyzable bond), no photolysis, no pH-driven degradation in beverage range. Maillard: as a NON-reducing triol it has no carbonyl group, so it does NOT participate in Maillard browning with amino acids/B6 (contrast dextrose/ribose). Hygroscopic — dry/neat glycerin will absorb atmospheric water (dilutes %). Microbially, glycerin at high concentration lowers water activity and is bacteriostatic, but at the ~1% diluted level here it provides NEGLIGIBLE preservative benefit and can serve as a carbon source for microbes in a wet, near-neutral matrix; preservation must come from K-sorbate (borderline at pH 6) or the dry/make-fresh design. Realistic shelf-life: glycerin itself is multi-year stable; the limiting factor is the rest of the wet matrix, not glycerin. Storage: avoid mild steel tanks (causes deterioration); keep closed (hygroscopic). Sources: Wikipedia Glycerol (acrolein on heating; hygroscopic); Cargill USP Excipient Glycerin datasheet (stable with water/ethanol/PG; steel-tank caution); US6784326B1 (pH oxidative-stabilization) (Tier 2, patent for the pH window only); Cargo Handbook Glycerin (hygroscopic, % reduction) (Tier 2).
- **R3 conflicts:** Glycerin is chemically inert toward the other formula actives — it has no acid/base, redox, or chelation reactivity and no carbonyl. Pairwise: (1) Glycerin + reducing sugars (dextrose, D-ribose, corn syrup, maltodextrin) -> mechanism: NONE; glycerin is a non-reducing triol and does not add to the Maillard load (it is, if anything, the inert co-solvent). Mitigation: none needed. (2) Glycerin + amino acids/amines (citrulline, taurine, betaine, P5P/B6) -> mechanism: NONE (no Schiff-base formation). (3) Glycerin + divalent metals (Mg/Ca/Zn) + citrate -> mechanism: NONE; glycerin does not chelate or precipitate; it does not affect existing Mg-citrate equilibria. (4) Glycerin + ascorbate/NAC (oxidation-prone thiols/enols) -> mechanism: NONE; glycerin is not a pro-oxidant and is not itself oxidized under storage; it does not catalyze ascorbate/NAC loss (unlike trace Fe/Cu). (5) Glycerin + K-sorbate preservative -> mechanism: as a metabolizable polyol/carbon source in a wet matrix glycerin can marginally support microbial growth if the sorbate barrier fails, but at ~1% it does not meaningfully shift water activity to help OR hurt; net effect minor. Mitigation: rely on adequate preservation (sorbate efficacy is already pH-limited) or the dry/make-fresh design which removes the question. Overall: glycerin is the formula's most benign ingredient compatibility-wise. Sources: Cargill USP Excipient Glycerin (chemically stable mixtures); Wikipedia Glycerol (non-reducing sugar alcohol); USP Monograph Glycerin (Tier 1/2).

## 3. Avian/poultry evidence
Glycerin is GRAS and a recognized energy substrate in poultry. Avian-specific data: AMEn of glycerin for broilers ~3,400-4,100 kcal/kg (e.g., ~3,916 kcal/kg DM for soybean-oil-industry glycerin, Dozier/Lima-type work; PMID 18212375 "Apparent metabolizable energy of glycerin for broiler chickens"; PMID 29112757 AMEn of soybean-oil by-products incl. glycerin). Optimum dietary inclusion ~5-6.7% as a partial energy source (PMID 21732883 "The effect of glycerol inclusion on broiler performance and nutrient digestibility"; PMID 25214552 source/level of glycerin on growth/liver/digestibility hatching-21d; PMC11205917 increasing glycerin levels in broilers). Glycerol is metabolized in liver via glycerol kinase to glycerol-3-phosphate -> gluconeogenesis (energy shortage) or lipogenesis (energy excess); above ~5% glycerol kinase saturates, capping its energy value. MECHANISM IN THIS FORMULA is NOT as a feed energy source — it is a sub-1% co-solvent/humectant (~few mg/bird/session), orders of magnitude below any feed inclusion level, so avian tolerability is non-limiting. No avian toxicity at the trace co-solvent dose used here. Acute vs chronic: feeding studies are chronic dietary (% of diet); the formula use is acute trace excipient — both regimes show glycerol is well tolerated/metabolizable in chickens. Sources: PMID 18212375; PMID 29112757; PMID 21732883; PMID 25214552; PMC11205917 (Tier 1).

## 4. Verdict
ADD-conditional (excipient role only). Glycerin passes R1 (infinitely water-miscible — no solubility limit), R2 (exceptionally stable in aqueous near-neutral matrix; no hydrolysis/oxidation/Maillard/photolysis at use conditions), and R3 (chemically inert toward all other actives). It is avian-tolerated and metabolizable. KEEP as a low-level (~1-2% of final drink) co-solvent/humectant/palatability aid IF any wet/liquid SKU is retained. In the WINNING dry-first redesign, the wet liquids (S2-S5, F5) are deleted, so glycerin is NOT needed and should be DROPPED from the dry preload and dry fight-day stick (a dry powder/stick has no need for a liquid humectant). Net recommendation: SKIP in the two-dry-SKU final design; ADD-conditional only as a benign co-solvent if a wet bottled product is ever forced (it is not the cause of any wet-product defect — the preservative/Maillard problems come from sorbate-at-pH-6 and reducing sugars, not glycerin). Dose if used wet: ~1-2.5 mL per 100 mL concentrate (~0.5-1.5% final after 1:3 dilution).

## 5. Cautions
- WELFARE/SAFETY: Glycerin is GRAS, non-toxic, non-irritating at the trace excipient level used; no welfare concern at this dose. Acrolein is only generated on strong HEATING (pyrolysis, >~280C) — irrelevant to a cool drink; do not heat glycerin-containing concentrates. - PRODUCIBILITY: Glycerin is hygroscopic; neat glycerin and any glycerin-containing concentrate will absorb atmospheric moisture, slowly diluting actives and potentially aiding microbial growth in a wet, under-preserved matrix — keep containers closed; this is a (minor) argument FOR the dry design. - Glycerin is a metabolizable carbon source; in a wet near-neutral product with marginal K-sorbate it does not improve microbial safety at ~1% (too dilute to lower water activity meaningfully) and the make-fresh/dry approach sidesteps the issue. - Avoid storing glycerin or its concentrates in mild-steel vessels (causes deterioration); use food/pharma-grade plastic or stainless. - Note: glycerin's value here is purely cosmetic/palatability/mouthfeel + humectant; it carries no acute ergogenic or hydration benefit for the bird, so deleting it in the dry redesign costs nothing functionally.
