# Substance: Potassium citrate (tripotassium citrate monohydrate)

# Substance Dossier: Potassium citrate (tripotassium citrate monohydrate)

## 1. Identity
Tripotassium citrate monohydrate. Formula K3C6H5O7·H2O (anhydrous K3C6H5O7). CAS 6100-05-6 (monohydrate); 866-84-2 (anhydrous). MW ~324.41 (monohydrate), ~306.4 (anhydrous). Synonyms: potassium citrate tribasic, tri-potassium citrate, E332(ii), citric acid tripotassium salt. White hygroscopic granular powder / transparent prismatic crystals; slightly alkaline. Compendial excipient with USP-NF and Ph.Eur. monographs; FDA GRAS (21 CFR 184.1625). In this formula it appears as the K-citrate component across S2 (2 g/100 mL), S3 (0.75 g/120 mL), S4 (1.2 g/250 mL), S5 (1 g/200 mL), F5 (0.5 g/150 mL). Sources: ChemicalBook CB6280529; Wikipedia Potassium citrate; CAS Common Chemistry 6100-05-6 (all Tier 2).

## 2. Three-rule findings
- **R1 solubility:** Aqueous solubility ~1500 g/L at 20C (Tier 2: American Elements / Merck Millipore 104956 datasheet), with a frequently cited value of 1.8 g per 1 mL (~1800 g/L) at 20C and rising to ~2 g/mL at 80C (Tier 2: ChemicalBook CB6280529 / chembk). Extremely freely water-soluble; no salt-form solubility issue. Intended in-solution concentration in current liquids ~5-20 g/L (highest S2 at 20 g/L concentrate, ~6.7 g/L after 1:3 dilution); in the winning dry fight-day drink design, citrate is supplied mainly as Na-citrate at only 0.3 g/L. Headroom at the worst case (20 g/L concentrate) = 1500/20 = ~75x. Verdict: OK (headroom >> 3). Solubility is never the limiting factor for potassium citrate.
- **R2 stability:** Chemically stable as a solid at ambient temperature; loses water of hydration at ~180C (Tier 2: ChemicalBook CB6280529; chembk). In aqueous solution citrate is hydrolytically and oxidatively robust — no ester/amide bonds to hydrolyze, not a reducing sugar (no Maillard), not photolabile, not redox-active itself. The salt is stable across the whole formula pH window (~5.8-6.2) and far beyond; a 1% solution reads pH 7.5-9.0 (Tier 2: chembk), so it is a net alkalizer/buffer, not a degradant. Realistic shelf-life of a dry blend is long (~12-18+ mo) if kept dry. KEY STABILITY DEFECT is NOT the citrate itself but its consequences in the wet products: (a) it is very hygroscopic/deliquescent in the monohydrate form — must be foil/desiccant packaged dry, otherwise it cakes and draws moisture that then drives degradation of co-formulated actives (Tier 2: Merck Millipore 104956; Carl Roth SDB_X888). (b) As an alkalizing buffer it raises local pH toward 6-7+, which (i) pushes the K-sorbate preservative further past its pKa ~4.76 (only ~6% undissociated active near pH 6, worse above) reducing antimicrobial efficacy, and (ii) accelerates pH-sensitive co-actives (P5P dephosphorylation optimum pH 6-6.5; methyl-B12 photolysis peaks ~pH 5; ascorbate oxidation faster near neutral). Citrate itself is microbiologically a usable carbon source, so a near-neutral citrate solution without effective preservative is at microbial risk. The dry-first design removes all of these by eliminating standing water. Sources: ChemicalBook CB6280529 (Tier 2); Merck Millipore 104956 / Carl Roth SDS (Tier 2); sorbic acid pKa ~4.76 standard physchem constant (Tier 2).
- **R3 conflicts:** Citrate is a tricarboxylate and a STRONG chelator of divalent cations (more potent than ascorbate/lactate/gluconate/fumarate/succinate/malate) — UChicago Kidney Stone Program (Tier 2); CRRT citrate reviews PMC10006386, PMC11313640 (Tier 1/peer-reviewed). Pairwise: (1) K-citrate + Mg-malate/MgCl2 (S5 Mg ~619 mg; S2/S3 Mg-malate; preload Mg) -> citrate chelates Mg2+ forming soluble Mg-citrate complexes; at the dilute formula concentrations this keeps Mg in solution (anti-precipitation, generally helpful) but can shift the free/ionized Mg fraction. Mechanism: ionic complexation. Mitigation: tolerable in solution but DO NOT co-concentrate citrate with divalent metals at high strength, and keep Mg in feed (winning design) where the relevant ceiling is enteric. (2) K-citrate + any Ca source -> Ca-citrate is only sparingly soluble; if calcium were ever added at higher strength, citrate could form Ca-citrate haze/precipitate. No Ca in current formula, so currently inert; flag for future Ca additions. (3) ACID-BASE / DEB conflict (the dominant issue): each citrate is metabolized to ~3 bicarbonate equivalents, so K-citrate is a strong net ALKALI load that counts fully positive in DEB (Na+K-Cl). The heavy multi-product citrate stack over-alkalinizes against the exerted bird's panting respiratory ALKALOSIS, risking alkalosis overshoot, and supplies almost no chloride (poor Cl-balance). Mechanism: metabolic bicarbonate generation + DEB skew. Mitigation: replace most citrate with Cl-containing salts (KCl for K, NaCl for Na) to hit a near-neutral, Cl-balanced drink; retain only a token ~0.3 g/L Na-citrate as a minimal buffer, as in the winning design. (4) K-citrate + K-sorbate -> alkalizing buffer lifts pH and cuts sorbate efficacy (see R2). Mitigation: dry/make-fresh removes dependence on sorbate. (5) K-citrate raises total K load — sum K across products vs session cap; in winning drink K ~15 mM (~55 mg/session) is well within limits.

## 3. Avian/poultry evidence
Potassium is an essential macromineral for poultry: NRC Nutrient Requirements of Poultry sets ~0.30% K (broiler starter) as requirement (Tier 1: NRC poultry; corroborated by review, Cambridge WPSJ "Electrolytes, dietary electrolyte balance and salts in broilers", Tier 2). Optimal dietary electrolyte balance DEB = Na+K-Cl ~186-250 mEq/kg feed, with 0-300 mEq/kg considered appropriate; predicted optimal K ~0.47-0.52% in starter/grower (Tier 1 peer-reviewed: Borges PMID 12619809; Moura/insights of DEB under heat stress PMID 39399982; review Tier 2). K and Cl supplementation is specifically beneficial in heat-stressed broilers (PMID 12619809). However, this evidence supports POTASSIUM as a nutrient and DEB management — it does NOT specifically endorse the CITRATE counter-ion for an acute fight-day drink. Citrate's alkalizing action is NOT avian-evidenced as beneficial during exercise; the avian frame (panting respiratory alkalosis + glycolytic lactate acidosis = mixed disturbance) argues AGAINST further alkali loading and FOR a near-neutral Cl-balanced drink. So K is justified; the citrate salt form for acute use is not avian-supported. For chronic feed DEB, K-citrate is an acceptable K/alkali source within the 150-300 mEq/kg target. No avian study isolates K-citrate vs KCl efficacy in gamefowl recovery — that specific comparison is UNKNOWN.

## 4. Verdict
SKIP for the fight-day drink / acute liquids (S2-S5, F5) — replace K-citrate with KCl. Rationale: solubility and intrinsic stability are fine (R1/R2 pass on the salt itself), but the citrate counter-ion is a strong alkali/DEB-skewing, Cl-poor, sorbate-efficacy-lowering, divalent-chelating load that fights the bird's panting respiratory alkalosis and contributes to the over-alkalinizing citrate stack. The winning design correctly moves K to KCl (drink K ~15 mM via KCl 1.1 g/L) and keeps only a token ~0.3 g/L Na-citrate as minimal buffer. ADD-conditional ONLY as a chronic DEB/alkali lever in the DRY PRELOAD feed if a measured DEB shortfall needs raising toward 150-300 mEq/kg — and even there KCl/K2CO3/KHCO3 choice should be made on the Cl/acid-base target, not defaulted to citrate. Dose if used in feed: titrate K to keep total dietary K ~0.5% and DEB <=300 mEq/kg; in the acute drink, K-citrate dose = 0 (use KCl). Not a solubility/stability BLOCK; it is a physiological/DEB-fit SKIP.

## 5. Cautions
1) Strong ALKALI/DEB load: each citrate yields ~3 bicarbonate equivalents — stacking K-citrate + Na-citrate across five products over-alkalinizes against the exerted bird's respiratory alkalosis; risk of alkalosis overshoot and Cl deficit. Prefer Cl-balanced KCl/NaCl. 2) Very hygroscopic/deliquescent monohydrate — must be foil/desiccant packaged; moisture uptake cakes the powder and seeds degradation of moisture-sensitive co-actives (NAC, B12). 3) Raises solution pH (1% soln pH 7.5-9.0), pushing K-sorbate past its effective range (pKa ~4.76) and accelerating P5P/B12/ascorbate loss in wet products — a key reason to go dry. 4) Citrate chelates Mg/Ca; helpful (keeps Mg soluble) at dilute strength but never co-concentrate citrate with divalent metals, and watch free-Mg shifts; Ca-citrate can haze if Ca is ever added. 5) Total K must be summed across all products vs session/feed caps; uricotelic birds excrete K excess renally so moderate excess is low-risk, but chronic high K with low Cl skews DEB. 6) Welfare note (non-blocking): K is a genuine, welfare-positive electrolyte for heat-stressed birds; the concern is purely the citrate counter-ion's alkalizing fit, not potassium itself.
