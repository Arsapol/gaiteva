# Substance: Methylcobalamin (vitamin B12, mecobalamin)

# Substance Dossier: Methylcobalamin (vitamin B12, mecobalamin)

## 1. Identity
Methylcobalamin; synonyms mecobalamin, MeCbl, MeB12, methyl-B12. The methylated bioactive cobalamin coenzyme form. CAS 13422-55-4. Molecular formula C63H91CoN13O14P, MW ~1344.4 g/mol. Dark red crystals forming cherry-red transparent aqueous solutions. Related vitamers: cyanocobalamin (CAS 68-19-9, the synthetic, more shelf-stable form used as standard feed B12), hydroxocobalamin, adenosylcobalamin. In this formula it appears as "Methyl-B12 5000mcg" in S3 Reset B (per 120 mL), S4 Oxygen NUSAVA B-complex (5 mL/250 mL), and F5 ATP Express (5000 mcg/150 mL).

## 2. Three-rule findings
- **R1 solubility:** Intended in-solution concentration is extremely low: 5000 mcg (5 mg) per 120 mL in S3 = ~0.042 mg/mL; per 150 mL in F5 = ~0.033 mg/mL; after 1:3 use-dilution ~0.008-0.010 mg/mL. Methylcobalamin is freely soluble in water ("freely form cherry-colored transparent solutions in water", Wikipedia/PubChem Tier 2; the close analog cyanocobalamin has documented aqueous solubility ~12.5 mg/mL = 1/80 g/mL, DrugBank/monograph Tier 1-2). Headroom = ~12500 mcg/mL solubility / ~42 mcg/mL intended = ~300x. Solubility is NEVER limiting for B12 at vitamin micro-doses. Verdict R1: OK (massive headroom, >300x). EXACT methylcobalamin mg/mL aqueous solubility from a pharmacopeial monograph is UNKNOWN (only the cyanocobalamin 12.5 mg/mL figure and qualitative "freely soluble" for MeCbl are sourced), but this gap is immaterial given the 300x+ margin.
- **R2 stability:** Dominant degradation pathway in water is PHOTOLYSIS, and methylcobalamin is the LEAST photostable common vitamer. Aerobic aqueous photolysis (pH 1-12) yields aquocobalamin in acid and hydroxocobalamin in alkali; apparent first-order photolysis rate constants range 2.80 (pH 12) to 27.25x10^-2 min^-1, with the MAXIMUM photolysis rate at pH ~5.0 (Ahmad et al., J Photochem Photobiol A/Sci Direct S1010603018302545, Tier 1). RP-HPLC stability-indicating study (Amer 2022, PMID 35670513, Tier 1): photodegradation followed zero-order kinetics with half-life ~0.99 h at 1971.53 lux; most stable at pH 5, least stable at pH 2; pronounced susceptibility to acidic, alkaline AND photolytic stress. Comparative photodegradation: MeCbl far less photostable than cyanocobalamin; cyanocobalamin/hydroxocobalamin are the most stable. Light-protected stability: extemporaneous MeCbl injections stable >=181 days at room temp ONLY in amber vials protected from light (PMID 31085783, Tier 1). CRITICAL CONFLICT WITH THIS FORMULA: the liquids sit at pH 5.8-6.2, i.e. on the high-rate shoulder just past the pH~5 photolysis maximum, and are pigmented translucent solutions with no guaranteed opacity -> worst-case photolytic loss of the active coenzyme over realistic handling/storage. Thermal/oxidative loss compounds this. R2 verdict for any LIQUID at this pH: BLOCK / loss-prone active. Dry preload is the clean fix.
- **R3 conflicts:** Pairwise conflicts with co-formulated ingredients: (1) Methylcobalamin + sodium ASCORBATE / ascorbic acid (S2 6g ascorbate; vitamin-C-containing B-complex): ascorbate accelerates cobalamin destruction (well-documented vitamin C -> B12 degradation; cobalamins are redox-labile) -> avoid co-solution; mitigation: separate, keep B12 dry. (2) Methylcobalamin + trace transition metals (Fe/Cu contaminants, and the Mg/electrolyte load): metal ions catalyze cobalamin photo-oxidation and free-radical degradation -> mitigation: chelator/sequestrant, low-metal water, dry storage. (3) Methylcobalamin + thiols (NAC): thiols can reductively cleave the Co-C bond and reduce cobalt; B12 + thiol in solution = mutual instability -> mitigation: never co-dissolve B12 with NAC (another reason both go dry). (4) Light + near-neutral pH (formula 5.8-6.2): not an ingredient but the matrix itself maximizes photolysis. No precipitation/chelation hard-stop with citrate. Pair -> mechanism -> mitigation all point to the same fix: B12 belongs in a dry, opaque, metal-/ascorbate-/thiol-free premix.

## 3. Avian/poultry evidence
Poultry B12 REQUIREMENT: ~20 mcg/kg feed meets growing chicken/duck requirement (Halle & Henning broiler/Pekin duck study; NRC Nutrient Requirements of Poultry 1994 lists ~3-10 mcg/kg for chickens; typical commercial supplementation 5-20 mcg/kg feed). B12 is an ESSENTIAL vitamin for poultry — birds cannot synthesize adequate B12 (gut synthesis insufficient; cobalt-dependent), so dietary supplementation is the standard route (Merck Vet Manual, Tier 1; MDPI Animals 2019 PMC6770686 Pekin-duck cyanocobalamin growth study, Tier 1). MECHANISM: methionine synthase / methylmalonyl-CoA mutase cofactor — methyl-group metabolism, supports the betaine/methionine methylation pathway already targeted by the formula's betaine. ACUTE vs CHRONIC: B12 is a slow-turnover, hepatically-stored vitamin repleted over days/weeks — it is a CHRONIC status nutrient, NOT an acute intra-fight ergogenic; a between-rounds sip delivers no acute benefit. MAX TOLERABLE: B12 is among the LEAST toxic vitamins; NRC Vitamin Tolerance of Animals lists no defined upper neurotoxic limit. FORM: for dry feed repletion, CYANOCOBALAMIN is the standard, more photostable, cheaper, and adequate form — the methyl form is not specifically required for status repletion. DOSE in formula: 5000 mcg/serving is ~250-1000x the daily poultry requirement — pharmacologic and wasted via renal clearance, plus the costly methyl form is the one most degraded by the wet matrix.

## 4. Verdict
SKIP methylcobalamin in ALL liquids (S3, S4 B-complex, F5, and any revised liquid drink) — BLOCK in the wet matrix because the formula pH 5.8-6.2 sits on the high-rate shoulder just past the pH~5 photolysis maximum for the least-photostable vitamer, and it co-resides with ascorbate/NAC/metals that degrade it. ADD-conditional to the DRY PRELOAD only, preferably substituting CYANOCOBALAMIN (more photostable, cheaper, adequate for poultry status). Recommended dose: dry feed premix targeting ~20-50 mcg B12/bird/day (a modest multiple of the ~20 mcg/kg-feed requirement; bird eats ~120-150 g/day), as cyanocobalamin in an opaque/foil, desiccated micro-premix with 2-step geometric dilution for uniformity. Net: delete the 5000 mcg liquid B12 line items; consolidate B12 to one dry source. This removes a BLOCK-severity stability defect with zero loss of avian benefit (B12 is chronic-status, not acute).

## 5. Cautions
Welfare/producibility notes (non-blocking): (1) 5000 mcg/serving is pharmacologic overkill vs ~20 mcg/kg-feed poultry requirement — not a toxicity hazard (B12 essentially non-toxic, renally cleared in uricotelic birds) but a cost/labeling waste and signals dose-by-guess formulation. (2) The methyl form is a deliberate cost-and-stability disadvantage vs cyanocobalamin; for dry feed status repletion cyano is standard and adequate — confirm no specific methyl-form requirement before substituting (there is none for status). (3) If any legacy B12 liquid is retained, it MUST be amber/opaque, cold/dark stored, kept away from ascorbate/NAC/trace metals, and made fresh; even then realistic photolytic loss makes label claim unreliable. (4) Methyl-B12 marketing ("active form", "energy") is Tier-3 hype with no avian acute-performance evidence; do not let it justify the liquid line items.
