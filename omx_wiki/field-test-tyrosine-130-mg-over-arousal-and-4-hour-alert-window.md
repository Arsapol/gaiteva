# Field Test — Tyrosine 130 mg Over-arousal and 4-hour Alert Window

# Field Test — Tyrosine 130 mg Over-arousal and 4-hour Alert Window

## Observation — 2026-06-18 operator report

Field-tested L-tyrosine around 130 mg in a fight-context bird. Result: bird became very alert/over-driven, jumped/attacked continuously, but accuracy/precision decreased early. After spending substantial energy, by round 3 the bird's accuracy improved again. Separate non-fighting bird given tyrosine showed alertness for approximately 4 hours.

## Interpretation

This is treated as high-value field evidence that 130 mg tyrosine can exceed the focus sweet spot in some phenotypes: drive/arousal rises before precision, timing, or accuracy. The round-3 improvement is consistent with arousal dropping back toward a better performance zone after early energy expenditure, not proof that higher tyrosine is universally better.

## Formula implication

- Do not use 130 mg tyrosine as a universal default.
- Phenotype-based tyrosine dosing is required: lower for naturally hot/aggressive birds, higher only for dull/late-start birds after test.
- A practical default target is closer to ~65-90 mg tyrosine per capsule; 100-110 mg only for low-drive birds; 130 mg reserved for confirmed low-drive phenotypes.
- Because alertness can last ~4 hours, avoid routine tyrosine re-dosing mid-fight. If a round-3 booster is needed, prefer non-tyrosine recovery/electrolyte/antioxidant support.
- Reassess phenylalanine: its intended role as delayed upstream tyrosine for later rounds is plausible in concept, but it may also add early LNAA competition and delayed catecholamine drive. Keep as a hypothesis requiring A/B field testing rather than a default drop without testing.
