# Substance: Trehalose (alpha,alpha-trehalose dihydrate; candidate stabilizer sugar)

# Substance Dossier: Trehalose (alpha,alpha-trehalose dihydrate; candidate stabilizer sugar)

## 1. Identity
Trehalose = alpha-D-glucopyranosyl-(1->1)-alpha-D-glucopyranoside, a NON-REDUCING disaccharide of two alpha-glucose units linked 1,1-glycosidically. Anhydrous: C12H22O11, MW 342.30 g/mol, CAS 99-20-7. Dihydrate (the normal commercial crystalline form): C12H22O11.2H2O, MW 378.33 g/mol, CAS 6138-23-4. Synonyms: D-(+)-trehalose, alpha,alpha-trehalose, mycose, mushroom sugar. Odorless white crystals, ~45% sweetness of sucrose. (Tier 2: Wikipedia/Trehalose; Tier 3: ChemicalBook CB4454890, BOC Sciences 304806.)

## 2. Three-rule findings
- **R1 solubility:** Aqueous solubility ~68.9 g/100 g water at 20 C, i.e. ~689 mg/mL (Tier 2: Wikipedia/Trehalose). Less soluble than sucrose below ~80 C but freely water-soluble at the concentrations any ORS/syrup would use. INTENDED USE in this formula: as a stabilizer/bulking sugar it would only ever be needed at low single-digit % (e.g. 1-5 g/L in a drink, or simply as a dry co-solute / lyo-bulking agent in the dry SKUs). Headroom vs an intended ~5-50 mg/mL in-solution use = ~14x to ~138x. >>3 => OK (no solubility constraint). Note the dihydrate is the crystalline grade; it carries ~9.5% water of crystallization, which is irrelevant once dissolved but matters for dry-blend moisture (see Cautions). Verdict R1: OK.
- **R2 stability:** Trehalose is among the MOST stable sugars in solution. (1) NON-REDUCING: the two anomeric carbons are joined in the 1,1 linkage, so it has no free reducing group and effectively does NOT participate in Maillard browning with amines/amino acids/B6 — model system brown-color rate constants were ~200-2000x LOWER for trehalose than sucrose at pH 2.5 (O'Brien 1996, J Food Sci 61(4):679-682, DOI 10.1111/j.1365-2621.1996.tb12180.x, Tier 1). (2) ACID/THERMAL: the 1,1-glycosidic bond resists acid hydrolysis; trehalose is stable in hot/acidic solution. This means at the formula dry-acidified target (drink pH ~3.8-4.2) and at the wet-formula pH band (~5.8-6.2) trehalose does NOT hydrolyze to glucose appreciably and does NOT brown. No photo/oxidative lability. (3) High glass-transition Tg => excellent DRY-STATE lyo/cryo-protectant and glassy-matrix former, protecting co-dried labile actives (B12, P5P, NAC thiol) by water-replacement/vitrification (Tier 1: PMC11510631 Lyoprotectants in Protein Stabilization; PMC11129304 / J Phys Chem B 10.1021/acs.jpcb.4c00022 Sucrose vs Trehalose by DSC). Stable pH window: broad, comfortably spans both 3.8-4.2 and 5.8-6.2. Shelf-life: effectively indefinite in solution chemically; the ONLY shelf risk is microbial (it is a fermentable sugar) and water uptake of the dihydrate — both solved by the dry/make-fresh design. Verdict R2: EXCELLENT STABILITY; non-reducing, non-browning, acid-stable, photostable, high-Tg glassy former.
- **R3 conflicts:** Pairwise vs current/redesigned ingredients: (a) vs reducing sugars + amino acids/B6 (the formula's worst Maillard stack: S2 corn syrup+maltodextrin+ribose+Na-ascorbate): trehalose is non-reducing and would REPLACE a reducing sugar -> mechanism: removes a Maillard reactant -> net stability benefit (O'Brien 1996 Tier 1). (b) vs dextrose/glucose in the fight-day ORS: no conflict, but note trehalose is poorly absorbed intact via SGLT1 and must be cleaved by intestinal trehalase to 2 glucose first; it does NOT substitute for dextrose as the SGLT1 cotransport driver (the drink's glucose:Na ratio must stay built on free glucose) -> mechanism: trehalase-dependent uptake, slower osmotically -> mitigation: keep dextrose as the SGLT1 sugar; use trehalose only as a stabilizer/bulking sugar, not as the rehydration carbohydrate. (c) vs divalent metals (Mg/Ca/Zn) + citrate/phosphate: trehalose is non-ionic, non-chelating -> NO precipitation/redox interaction; it can co-exist with electrolytes without binding. (d) vs ascorbate/NAC-thiol oxidation (Fe/Cu catalysis): trehalose is redox-inert, neither accelerates nor protects against metal-catalyzed oxidation in solution (its protection is a DRY glassy-matrix effect, not an aqueous antioxidant effect) -> in the dry premix it helps preserve B12/P5P/NAC by vitrification. (e) Osmolality: each g/L adds ~2.6 mOsm/kg (MW 342) — at 5 g/L only ~15 mOsm/kg, negligible vs the <300 cap. Verdict R3: COMPATIBLE; no conflicts; preferred over reducing sugars as a dry-blend co-solute.

## 3. Avian/poultry evidence
DIRECT AVIAN DATA EXISTS (Tier 1). Yu et al. 2025, "Trehalose improves growth performance and gut health of yellow-feathered broilers exposed to heat stress," Tropical Animal Health and Production 57:414, DOI 10.1007/s11250-025-04665-4. Design: 240 day-old broilers, 4 groups — thermoneutral control, heat-stress + basal (HS0, 33+/-2 C), HS + 0.1% dietary trehalose, HS + 0.5% dietary trehalose. Findings: 0.5% trehalose raised 28-d body weight vs HS0/HS0.1; reversed the heat-stress-induced drop in serum total antioxidant capacity and rise in malondialdehyde (lipid peroxidation marker); mitigated the heat-stress rise in ALT at 56 d; modulated gut microbiota. So as a DIETARY (dry feed) additive at ~0.5% of feed (~5 g/kg feed; ~0.6-0.75 g/bird/day at 120-150 g intake) trehalose is avian-evidenced for heat-stress antioxidant/gut-health support and growth — a CHRONIC, feed-delivered mechanism, NOT an acute drink lever. No avian data found for trehalose as an in-water ORS sugar or for acute single-session benefit; that use would be as a formulation stabilizer only, not an evidenced active. Poultry intestinal trehalase activity is the rate-limit on systemic glucose yield from trehalose (UNKNOWN quantitative chicken trehalase Vmax — see blocking gaps).

## 4. Verdict
ADD-conditional, in TWO narrow roles, mostly the DRY PRELOAD: (1) PRELOAD (dry feed premix): include trehalose at ~0.5% of feed (~5 g/kg feed, ~0.6-0.75 g/bird/day) as an avian-evidenced chronic heat-stress antioxidant/gut-health adjunct AND as a dry glassy-matrix stabilizer/carrier that protects co-blended labile actives (B12, P5P, NAC) by vitrification — this is where the real evidence and the real stabilizer value land (Yu 2025; PMC11510631). (2) FORMULATION STABILIZER, optional: if any sugar bulking/anti-browning is wanted in a dry stick, trehalose is the correct non-reducing choice over corn syrup/maltodextrin/ribose. SKIP it as the fight-day ORS rehydration carbohydrate — keep DEXTROSE for SGLT1; trehalose needs trehalase cleavage and is not the cotransport sugar. Do NOT add to wet liquids for "stability" — its protection is a dry-state effect, and the redesign deletes the liquids anyway. Net: a genuinely useful, very-stable, non-Maillard sugar whose best home is the dry preload, secondarily a dry-stick bulking aid.

## 5. Cautions
Notes (non-blocking): (1) Trehalose is a fermentable sugar — in any reconstituted wet drink it supports microbial growth identically to other sugars; rely on the make-fresh-daily / acidified / discard-after-session discipline. (2) Dihydrate grade carries ~9.5% crystal water and is mildly hygroscopic in bulk; in a dry premix store desiccated/foil so it does not cake or donate moisture to NAC/B12. NOTE: PMC11510631 flags that trehalose dihydrate crystallization can destabilize formulations on frozen storage — sucrose sometimes added to suppress it; pack airtight/desiccated. (3) NOT a free-glucose source on its own — uptake requires intestinal trehalase; do not count its sugar toward SGLT1 rehydration math or toward immediate energy delivery. (4) The avian benefit is at a CHRONIC ~0.5% feed dose over days; an acute in-drink sip has no evidenced benefit. (5) Welfare-positive: evidenced effect is the bird's OWN antioxidant capacity, lower lipid peroxidation, lower ALT, and better gut health under heat stress. (6) Human-medicine signal: Collins et al. 2018 (Nature 553:291-294, PMID 29310122) showed high oral trehalose can expand toxigenic C. difficile lineages in mammals (RT027/RT078 point mutation ~500x sensitivity) — UNKNOWN relevance to chickens and not at supplement-scale feed doses, but worth monitoring on gut-health scale-up.
